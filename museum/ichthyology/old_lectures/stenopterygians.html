The remaining superorders of euteleosts show a number of advancements over ostariophysans and protacanthopteryigians: they possess a retractor dorsalis muscle, which controls the pharyngeal jaws; they also have hinged teeth and a new rostral cartilage between the premaxillaries. Although not yet accorded a formal taxonomic rank, based on these characters some refer to these fishes as neoteleosts.

Neoteleosts are a difficult group to characterize based on external morphology. Fishes in the "intermediate superorders" possess a combination of ancestral and derived characteristics, and display a bizarre array of body forms, most reflecting adaptations to life in the deep sea.

Stenopterygians have characteristics that link them closer to protacanthopterygians than to acanthopterygians (i.e., adipose fin, cycloid scales, soft rayed fins, abdominal pectorals, toothed included maxilla). Cyclosquamata and Scopelomorphans, and Paracanthopterigians possess a mix of ancestral and derived characteristics. Lampridomorphs and Polymixomorphs were formerly classified with the spiny rayed fishes (Superorder Acanthopterygii) and much disagreement still exists on their placement.

Stenopterygians are deepsea predators that are usually black in color with luminescent organs (photophores) and large, heavily toothed jaws. They are assigned to a single order (Stomiiformes) with nine families and about 250 species.

Bristlemouths (Gonostomatidae) are the most common and widespread stomiiformes. These are small (usually less than 5 cm) mesopelagic fishes adapted for feeding on both large and small prey. Members of the genus Cyclothone are considered the most abundant fish in the world.

Hatchet fishes (Sternoptychidae) have deep, strongly compressed, hatchet shaped bodies that are silver in color with many, large photophores. The upturned mouth and often tubular eyes are adaptation for feeding on migratory zooplankton.

The other families of stomiiformes are mainly lie-in-wait predators adapted for feeding on large fish. Some have long chin barbels with a luminescent "lure" at the tip. In the family Idiacanthidae the males are small and function only in reproduction, and the larvae have long, stalked eyes.

Superorder Cyclosquamata comprises a single order (Aulopiformes) of marine fishes grouped in seven families. These are soft-rayed fishes, with physoclistic or absent swimbladder, excluded maxillae, adipose fin, and abdominal pelvic fins. Most of the fishes in this group posses photophores and live as deepsea predators, although one family (Synodontidae) occurs in shallow, inshore areas. Family Paralepidae includes small (less than 15 cm) fishes called barracudinas because they look like little barracudas. The lancetfishes (Alepisauridae) are large (up to 2m) mesopelagic predators. They may be the largest fishes in the mesopelagic zone.

Superorder Scopelomorpha comprises a single order (Myctophiformes) and two families of abundant marine mesopelagic fishes. Charateristics of this superorder reflect a similar mix of ancentral and derived traits as in the Cyclosquamata, and these fishes also possess photophores. Most of the species are lanternfishes (family Mytophidae). They occur in dense, multispecies schools that are often detected by sonar and misinterpreted as seabottom.

Superorder Paracanthopterygii comprises 4 orders of marine fishes and one order adapted to freshwater. This group is also characterized by mix of ancestral and derived traits, although most tend to have more derived than primitive features. Most workers doubt that all of the orders in this group share a more recent common ancestry to each than to other groups of fishes (i.e., is monophyletic).

The lone freshwater order (Percopsiformes) includes 3 families endemic to freshwaters of North America which exhibit a bizzare mix of characters. Troutperch (Percopsidae) have an adipose fin, ctenoid scales, and dorsal, anal and pelvic spines. Species in the family Amblyopsidae (cavefishes, 6 species) have cycloid scales, lack pelvic fins, and dorsal and anal spines are sometimes absent. The pirate perch (lone member of family Aphredoderidae) is essentially perch-like with a peculiar jugular anus.

The order Gadiformes includes over 400 species of bottom-oriented, commercially important marine species commonly called cod and hake. Members are characterized by elongate, tapering bodies, long dorsal and anal fins, thoracic or jugular pelvic fins, and cycloid scales.

In the cod family (Gadidae) the caudal fin is separate from the dorsal and anal fins, the fins lack spines, the swimbladder is physoclistic, and a chin barble is usually present. Cod are the dominant marine predators in the Artic region. One species, the burbot, is freshwater. True hake (Merrluciidae) differ from gadids in having a spine in the dorsal fin, no chin barbel, and a larger and more terminal mouth with sharp teeth (more piscivorous). Grenadiers (Macrouridae) are important benthic scavengers found in deepwater throughout the world.

Members of order Ophidiiformes are similar to grenadiers in appearance, but more elongate and eellike. The largest family is the Ophidiidae (cusk eels) with over 160 species found in a wide range of marine habitats. The brotulas (familes Bythitidae and Aphyonidae) are deepsea benthic and cave adapted forms with greatly reduced eyes. Some of the most remarkable ophidiiforms are the pearlfishes (Carapidae) which live as commensals or parasites inside sea cucumbers. They have elongate, slender bodies without caudal, anal and pelvic fins. Their life cycle includes planktonic and benthic larval stages.

Order Batrachoidiformes includes only one family, Batrachoididae (toadfishes) with about 65 species of shallow-water marine fishes. Toadfishes have squat, scaleless bodies with jugular pelvic fins and a toadlike head. They make loud sounds by vibrating their swimbladders.

The order Lophiiformes includes 16 families and over 265 species of anglerfishes, so-called because most have a fishing pole on their head (illicium) equipped with a lure at the end (esca) for attracting prey. The most familiar families (suborder Lophioidei) are substrate-oriented, cryptically colored, ambush predators, with small scales, jugular pelvic fins, no ribs, and physoclistic swimbladders (when present). In the lophioid families Antennariidae and Ogcocephalidae (frogfishes and batfishes), the pectoral fins are muscularized and arm like, and used for "walking".

A number of lophiid families (suborder Ceratoidei) are bathypelagic with large mouths and teeth and a luminescent esca. Males typically live as parasites on females and are for reproduction only.

Superorder Lampridomorpha comprises a single order (Lampridiformes) with as many as 11 families of mostly deepwater marine fishes with cycloid or absent scales, a physoclistic swimbladder, thoracic pelvics and a mobile maxilla. Four suborders are recognized. The most familiar fishes in this group are the opah (Family Lampridae), a large marine food fish, and the oarfish (Family Trachipteridae), a large, elongate fish with oar-like pelvics, and a dorsal fin that runs the entire length of the body and forms a large crest on the head. Oarfish reach 8 m in length and may have been the basis of sea-serpent stories.

Superorder Polymixomorpha includes a single order and family of tropical, marine, mid-water fishes called beardfishes. Some workers classify beardfishes as basal acanthopterygians. Others classify them as "position uncertain" below Acanthopteygians.
