The most derived euteleostian fishes are classified in the superorder Acanthopterygii ("spiny-rayed" fishes). Fishes in this group generally posses spiny-rayed fins; vertically aligned, midlateral pectorals; thoracic of jugalar pelvics; ctenoid scales; toothless excluded maxillae; swimbladder physoclistic or absent. The success of the acanthopterygians is attributed to the spiny rays in the fins which increase effective body diameter relative to body mass. Three lines of spiny-rayed fishes are distinguished at the series level: Atherinimorpha and Mugilomorpha are basal; whereas, Percomorpha is advanced.

Series Mugilomorpha includes a single order and family of marine and anadromous fishes commonly referred to as mullets (100 species). Mullets are schooling fishes that live in coastal areas and ascend rivers, feeding on microscopic plant and detrital material. The position of this group of fishes has been a matter of debate for decades. There is growing evidence that mullets, together with Atherinimorphs, Synbranchid eels, Gasterosteiforms, and pigmy sunfishes (Elassomatids) should be classified together as a group (the Smegamorpha) separate from other Acanthopterygians.

Atherinimorphs (series Atherinimorpha) are generally small, surface-oriented fishes with large eyes, flattened heads, upturned mouths, and dorsal fins set far back on the body. Te group is primarily marine, but species have invaded freshwater. Atherinimorphs are classified in three orders: Atheriniformes, Beloniformes and Cyprinodontiformes.

Cyprinodontiformes , characterized by a single dorsal fin and fins rarely with spines; and Atheriniformes, typically with two dorsal fins and spines present in fins. Two groups of cyprinodontiform fishes are recognized: the exocoetoids, and the cyprinodontoids.

Beloniforms are primarily marine, epipelagic fishes with roughly equal dorsal and anal fins located far back on the body, low-running lateral line, and abdominal pelvic fins. Two suborders are recognized: Exocoetoidei (needlefishes, halfbeaks, and relatives) and Adrianichthyoids (ricefishes). Halfbeaks (Hemiramphidae) and flyingfishes (Exocoetidae) are most closely related despite their morphological differences. The elongate lower jaws of halfbeaks help them to direct zooplankton into their mouths. Needlefishes (Belonidae) use their garlike beaks to catch small fish.

Ricefishes of the suborder Adrianichthyoidei are freshwater and marine fishes of Southeast Asia. Included in this group are the medakas of the genus Oryzias, widely regarded as the "white rats" of the fish world.

Cyprinodontiformes are primarily freshwater fishes, but most are capable of surviving a wide range of salinity and temperature conditions. Rivulines (Aplocheilidae) inhabit areas subject to drying and have drought resistant eggs. Some rivulines are hermaphroditic. The topminnows and killifishes (Family Fundulidae) are an important group of freshwater and euryhaline, neuston-feeding fishes. Toothcarps or pupfishes of the family Cyprinodontidae range from desert springs (Cyprinodon) to high mountain lakes (Orestias).

Members of the family Poeciliidae and three other families are livebearers; in males, the anal fin is modified into an intromittent organ, gonopodium. Mosquitofish (Gambusia) are important predators on mosquitos and have been dispersed worldwide as a biological control agent.

The order Atheriniformes comprises 230+ species of slender, diurnal, planktivorous fishes, most of which are silversides (family Atherinidae). Atherinids apparently gave rise to the deep-bodied rainbowfishes (Melanotaeniidae) of Austrailia and New Guinea, and the priapium fishes (Neostethidae) of Southeast Asian, the males of which have a complex copulatory organ (priaium) located in the throat region.
