Protacanthopterygians

Division Teleostei

  Subdivision Euteleostei

    Superorder Protacanthopterygii

      Order Esociformes (pikes, mudminnows)

      Order Salmoniformes (salmon, trout, whitefish)

      Order Osmeriformes (smelts, galxiids, argentioids)

 

Superorder Protacanthopterygii is a primitive group of euteleosts adapted to temperate and polar freshwater and marine habitats. Many are diadromous, showing both anadromy and catadromy. This group shares many characters in common with ostariophysans (abdominal pelvic fins, ventral pectorals, premaxilla present but not protractile, physostomus swimbladder, adipose fin). However, protacanthopterygians are more predatory, and are more popular as sport fish.

Order Esociformes includes 11 species in 3 families, all freshwater. Characteristics include posteriorly located dorsal fin (generally even with anal fin), adipose fin absent. Family Esocidae (pikes) with 5 species is characterized by a "ducklike" snout and an elongate, terete body. Most pikes are large lie-in-wait predators (good sport fish). One species (Esox lucius) has a circumpolar distribution.

Family Umbridae contains the small mudminnows (5 species) characterized by blunt snouts and rounded tails. Mudminnows live in habitats subject to anoxia and thus have limited air breathing abilities. They borrow in mud when frightened.

Family Dalliidae includes a single species, the Alaskan blackfish (Dallia pectoralis). The species inhabits arctic bogs and acid lakes that are subject to complete freezing; its blood is believed to have antifreeze quality to prevent ice crystal formation.

Order Salmoniformes comprises a single family of northern hemisphere, freshwater and anadromous species characterized by the presence of an adipose fin (usually), dorsal fin inserted midway on the body (generally even with the pelvic fin).

Salmonids (family Salmonidae) are the dominate fishes in cold subarctic freshwaters. Three distinct lines are recognized (subfamilies): salmon, trout and char (Salmoninae); grayling (Thymallinae); and whitefishes (Coregoninae). Many salmon, trout and char are anadromous and generally reach larger sizes and produce more eggs than species that remain in freshwater. Salmonids presumably use streams for spawning because of reduced predation relative to the ocean. Some species alternate between freshwater and anadromous life histories (e.g., Salmo gairdneri and Onchorhychus mykiss).

Order Osmeriformes includes two rather distinct groups of fishes commonly referred to as smelts and argentioids (classified at the suborder level). Most are marine and tend to be smaller than salmoniformes.

Smelts (suborder Osmeroidei) are small diadromous and marine fishes found in the northern hemisphere (3 families). They mass in large numbers in streams and along beaches in large numbers for spawning. Galaxiids are freshwater and catadromous smelt-like fishes found only in the southern hemisphere. They are ecological equivalents of northern hemisphere trout and char, and superficially resemble these fishes (but lack an adipose fin).

Argentioids are deepwater, marine, smelt-like fishes. As many as nine families are recognized. Many have abnormally large eyes adapted for vision under low light. Barreleyes or spookfishes have upward directed eyes with many rods, which permit them to see at very low light.
