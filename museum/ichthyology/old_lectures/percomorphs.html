Series Percomorpha includes about half of all known fishes. All percomorphs conform to a common structural plan, which includes spines in the fins, thoracic pelvic fins, protractile premaxilla, ctenoid scales, and swimbladder physoclistic or absent. As a group, percomorphs are most successful in benthic inshore marine habitats, but a number of groups have successfully reinvaded freshwater. Their success is attributed to presence of fin spines, which increase effective body diameter relative to body mass; this presumably reduced the need for swimming speed or heavy body armor. Anterior insertion of pelvic fins and midlateral insertion of pectorals allowed greater maneuverability in swimming. The series contains 9 orders, 230 families and about 11,000 species.

 

Fishes in the orders Beryciformes, Stephanoberyciformes, and Zeiformes exhibit ancestral characters relative to other Percomorphs, are generally deepwater dwellers, and appear to be closely related (suggesting deepwater marine origin for all fishes in series Percomorpha).

Beryciformes are primarily deepsea fishes, although many members of the largest and most familiar family, Holocentridae (squirrelfishes), live in relativley shallow water. Stephanoberyciformes (8 families, 39 species) are an unusual group of deepwater fishes called whalefishes. Zeiformes (dories) are midwater to deepsea fishes with large heads and eyes.

Order Gasterosteiformes is a highly specialized group of primarily marine fishes with elongate snouts, small mouths and bony armor (Nelson, 1984, recognizes 4 orders). Stickelbacks (Gasterosteidae) are small, euryhaline, nest-building species with short snouts and reduced bony armor. Seamoths (Pegasidae, 5 species) are small, marine fishes that are completely encased in armor and have a ventral sucking mouth. Seahorses and pipefishes (Syngnathidae, 230+ species), are shallow-water marine and estuarine fishes with small, tube-like, sucking mouths, and complete bony armor.

 

Order Scorpaeniformes includes 20 families and over 1100 species of primarily marine, bottom-oriented fishes with large, spiny heads, and large rounded pectoral fins. The largest family is the Scorpaenidae, rockfishes and scorpionfishes (over 310 species). Tropical forms (e.g., tigerfish, stonefish) are extremely toxic. Searobins (Triglidae) are widely distributed marine fishes adapted for soft bottoms. They have large pectoral fins with three independent fingerlike projections. Cottidae (sculpins) is a large, primarily marine family (300+ species) adapted to turbulent water.

Dactylopterids (flying gurnards) resemble searobins. They do not actually fly but rather use their large pectoral fins to spook predators.

Perciformes is the largest vertebrate order (over 7800 species). These are all "perch-like" fishes (i.e., popular sport fish). Nineteen suborders are recognized.

Suborder Percoidea (percoids) comprises almost half of all perciforms. Most are large, inshore piscivores, which are important as sport fish. Seabasses (Serranidae, 370 species) are hermaphroditic (simultaneous or protogynous). Also included in this group are temperate basses (Percichthyidae), sunfishes (Centrarchidae), cardinalfishes (Apogonidae), perches (Percidae), cobias (Rachycentridae), remoras (Echeneidae), jacks (Carangidae), snappers (Lutjanidae), grunts (Haemulidae), drums (Sciaenidae), butterfly fishes (Chaetodontidae), surfperches (Embiotocidae), cichlids (Cichlidae), and damselfishes (Pomacentridae).

Barracudas (Sphyraenoids, one family) are a related group of marine piscivores. Labroids, wrasses (Labridae) and parrotfishes (Scaridae), are important reef fishes with a unique beaklike mouth.

Trachinoids are a group of burying, ambush predators with dorsal eyes and strongly upturned mouths (240 species in 15 families).

Blennioids are small, rock-dwelling, marine fishes with elongate, sculpin-like bodies (6 families, 675 species). Gobioids are small, primarily marine, bottom dwellers with elongated bodies and disc-like pelvic fins (7 families, 1700 species).

Fishes in suborder Gobiesocoidei also have their pelvic fins modified into a thoracic sucking disk which allows them to cling to rocks in the surf zone where they forage on algae and molluscs. The group comprises two families and 136 species.

Acanthuroids (2 families, 77 species) are deep-bodied, marine fishes with beaklike snouts, and scalpel-like spines near the tail (hence the common name surgeonfishes). Scombroids (7 families, 100 species) are large, epi- and mesopelagic piscivours, mackerels and tunas (Scombridae) and billfishes. Stromatoids (6 families, 65 species) are best known from the epipelagic juvenile stage which lives in association with jellyfishes.

Anabantoids (4 families, 70+ species) are surface-oriented, freshwater fishes called gouramis. They are adapted for air-breathing (labyrinthine organ) and many use air bubbles for hearing and reproduction.

 

Order Synbranchiformes (swamp eels) includes a single family of widespread, freshwater eels with pouch gills for airbreathing. They are protogynous hermaphrodites.

Order Pleuronectiformes (6 families, 530+ species) are marine fishes that are uniquely adapted for life on the bottom (flat and asymmetrical).

Order Tetraodontiformes (9 families, 330 species) are marine fishes with a variety of antipredator devices (trigging spines, puffing, poison, bony armor). They have fused chisel-like teeth for feeding on heavy-shelled invertebrates. Also included in this group are the large, pelagic molas (Molidae).
