How did you first become interested in ichthyology?
Inspired by Dr. David Etnier when I was an undergraduate research participate and student in his ichthyology course.

What steps did you have to take to get where you are today?
Secured a M.S. in Fisheries and Wildlife Science, worked for 5 years as a stream survey team biologist in Kentucky, returned for Ph.D. to work under Dr. Brooks Burr at Southern Illinois University, took a year-long post-doc with Dr. Paul Angermeier at Virginia Tech, took job as Research Biologist (emphasis aquatic conservation and stream ecology), ultimately took on Team Leader role in present position.

What kinds of skills are required for your career? What experiences do you feel prepared you best for your job?
The -ology courses (ichthyology, aquatic entomology, limnology, etc.) were foundational. Extensive experience in stream and small river surveys were essential. A broad graduate education, covering taxonomy (Ph.D.), conservation (endangered species work), working with game fish (M.S.), and stream ecology (GIS, zoogeography) (post-doc). I feel my experience in preparing and writing scientific papers (from notes to full-blown papers to book chapters to books) was a pivotal skill as well as a strong background in applied statistics. Participating in leadership positions (both societal and agency related) are experiences from which I have better developed my ability to coordinate people, reach goals, and produce research products.

What are your responsibilities?
I supervise 3 research scientists (two aquatic ecologists and one ornithologist), 3 biological technicians (up to 9 additional during summer), a computer programmer, and 2 administrative personnel. I am responsible for all aspects of our units program (funding, budget, physical plant, fleet, and research productivity) concerning aquatic and terrestrial ecology, the latter being handled primarily by one of my scientists (an ornithologist). I am also a Group Leader (Aquatic Ecology) for a large-scale ecosystem management research project in the Ouachita Mountains. I work with university researchers in designing and implementing research on the stream systems in that region. At this time, I am an Associate Editor for the North American Journal of Fisheries Management (will switch to Transactions of the American Fisheries Society this fall) and I am Science Editor for Fisheries. I enjoy editing.

What do you like best about your career?
I have an extraordinary amount of scientific freedom to pursue research questions and develop research programs. I am able to spend a lot of time in the field with generally adequate to excellent equipment, transportation, funding, and technical support. I am engaged professionally in societal participation (primarily AFS and ASIH) and enjoy that aspect of my job. I also generally enjoy my editing assignments. Lastly, I enjoy working with other bright, young scientists and helping them develop professionally.

What would be your most important piece of advice for someone considering the career you're in now?
Use graduate school time wisely. Pick well-conceived projects, finish them on time, and move on. If opportunities arise to do field work with others, do it. Field experience is paramount and the broader that experience (whether the tasks or the geography) the better.
