How did you first become interested in ichthyology?
At age 16 I knew I would be an ichthyologist, probably due to the years spend snorkeling and spearfishing on the reefs of Jamaica, my birth place.

What steps did you have to take to get where you are today?
Started by reading "The History of Fishes" at 16 years of age and never stopped planning my career. I worked at marine labs throughout my high school years and then conducted three years of independent study at Duke as an undergrad in Zoology. From there I continued my dream by taking as many related courses as possible and never turning down an opportunity to partake in fish research.

What kinds of skills are required for your career? What experiences do you feel prepared you best for your job?
Writing skills first and foremost, then critical thinking, reading of as many papers in the field as possible (get off the internet and read papers!), statistical ability, the ability to design and execute scientific projects, and ability to publish your work. No, SCUBA diving ability has little to do with your success so don't list it first on your resume.

What are your responsibilities?
Competent teaching at the undergraduate and graduate level, advising students at all levels, conducting scientific research, writing and publishing papers, writing grant proposals and securing external monies to conduct research, serving my department and university through committee work and the like, serving my societies such as ASIH and AES, and grading papers, writing letters of recommendation, and taking the time to fill out paperwork like this.

What do you like best about your career?
The ability to mentor graduate students and conduct research of my choice. Not to mention traveling to cool places, diving with my animals, drinking beer with my graduate students, and never having to punch a clock. I rest my case.

What would be your most important piece of advice for someone considering the career you're in now?
Never give up on your dream, but plan for it in the smallest detail. It's rarely luck that gets you into that dream job. Work and study hard, go the extra mile, get involved with research from an early age in your career, and start publishing your work when you're an undergraduate. Don't be fixed on one type of animal or study area too early, you will have plenty of time to do that later on. Get a broad undergraduate education in all areas of biology, and then in graduate school specialize but not to the point of myopia.
