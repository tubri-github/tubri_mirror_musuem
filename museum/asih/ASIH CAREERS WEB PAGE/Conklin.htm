Name: Dan Conklin
Current Position: Senior Biologist, The Florida Aquarium
Education: B.S. Botany, University of South Florida, FL
Website: http://www.flaquarium.net/
Favorite Fish: members of Clupeidae
Favorite Ice Cream: French vanilla
  


How did you first become interested in ichthyology?
I started keeping aquarium fish when I was about eight years old and had parents that let me have as many aquariums as my allowance allowed. I was also fortunate to grow up near the coast where snorkeling and fishing activities provided me with opportunities to observe fish in their neural habitats.

What steps did you have to take to get where you are today?
A keen interest made for a lot of self-training during my childhood. Much of the literature available to aquarium hobbyists has been derived from scientific studies and that became useful later on. A biology degree, although in botany, contributed a great deal to polishing that training. After graduating from USF I completed an internship and contributed many hours of volunteer service at The Florida Aquarium.

What kinds of skills are required for your career?
Aquarium husbandry skills are probably the most important. These skills are actually an accumulation of knowledge and experience. SCUBA certification and chemistry skills are also useful.

What experiences do you feel prepared you best for your job?
Practical experience with aquarium fish was important however I don't think I would be where I am without the academic training.

What are your responsibilities?
My primary responsibilities involve the quarantine of fish and other aquatic animals and the supervision of water quality control. Secondarily I also participate in the acquisition of specimens from the field and assist in research projects as needed.

What do you like best about your career?
I enjoy being able to work with aquatic life every day. There is some structure in my daily routine but most days present a different challenge to be solved.

What would be your most important piece of advice for someone considering the career you're in now?
An academic background in natural sciences is important. Obviously the more advanced the degree the better. It seems that the majority of public aquarists have undergraduate degrees while a few have post graduate level degrees. Advanced degrees often occupy management positions but rarely without years of experience. Any practical experience can go a long way in building credentials. Internships and volunteer time at an appropriate institution such as a public aquarium, university lab, or government agency are the most likely sources to get the foot in the door.
