How did you first become interested in ichthyology?
Whilst being an amateur fossil collector as a child, I realised that many people had done work on the fossil mammals and reptiles, but very few had looked at the fish fossils in my area, so I became very interested in them.

What steps did you have to take to get where you are today?
I did an Honours year (4th year) geology thesis on the placoderm fish Bothriolepis from Victoria, Australia, then went on to do a Ph.D on the extraordinary Middle Devonian Mt Howitt fossil fish fauna (also from Victoria), which contained many new species of placoderms, acanthodians,
lungfishes, osteolepiforms, actinopterygians and a primitive coelacanth.

What kinds of skills are required for your career? What experiences do you feel prepared you best for your job?
A combination of studying zoology and geology at University, plus an early gained amateur knowledge of palaeontology from my childhood fossil collecting days.

What are your responsibilities?
I manage the fossil vertebrate collections, undertake researh primarily on fossil fishes (including regular fieldwork collecting specimens both locally and internationally), I provide information to Government and the public about fossils, and often give public or academic lectures about palaeontology.

What do you like best about your career?
Fieldwork- the thrill of discovering new species that provide important information about the evolution of early fishes.

What would be your most important piece of advice for someone considering the career you're in now?
Think carefully before embarking on a palaeontological career-job prospects are always minimal. But, if you are prepared to work hard, and publish lots of good research papers, jobs can always be created through research grants for good palaeontologists. It is a very satisfying and interesting career.
