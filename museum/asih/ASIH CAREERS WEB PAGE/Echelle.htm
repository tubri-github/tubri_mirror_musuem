Name: Anthony A. Echelle
Current Position: Professor of Zoology, Oklahoma State University
Education: B.S. Southeastern Oklahoma State University, OK
M.S. & Ph.D. University of Oklahoma, OK
Website: http://zoology.okstate.edu/zoo_fclt/echelle.htm
Favorite Fish: Red River pupfish
Favorite Ice Cream: Lowfat chocolate

 

How did you first become interested in ichthyology?
I could have gone into the study of about any group because of a general interest in natural history. I got into fishes because of a fellowship opportunity at The University of Oklahoma with Carl Riggs, my advisor for the M.S. degree.

What steps did you have to take to get where you are today?
Starting a year before I finished the Ph.D., I took a vacated position as Research Biologist at the University of Oklahoma Biological Station. My wife (Alice Fitch Echelle) and I then spent six years as a research team at the station with no responsibilities except whatever research we chose to do. At that time, we were not into grantsmanship, and research was funded out of our own pockets. Still, our publication rate was greater than at anytime before or since. Probably because of that, I got a faculty position at Baylor University. We were there six years when I was offered my present position at Oklahoma State University. I was happy at Baylor and would not have applied for the OSU position were it not for Steve Lehtinen, a former M.S. student of mine who was the graduate-student representative on the OSU search committee and had asked me to apply.

What kinds of skills are required for your career? What experiences do you feel prepared you best for your job?
Required skills: Staying abreast of methods and approaches in data gathering and analysis is a continuing challenge.

Experiences:
1. Summers at the OU Biological Station where, as a graduate student, I was exposed to field biologists of all stripes
2. Herpetological fieldwork in 1970-71 with Alice and her father (Henry S. Fitch) in Mexico and Central America. This was my first exposure to fieldwork far-removed from home base.
3. A 2-month stay in 1972 at the Savannah River Ecology Laboratory where I worked with Michael H. Smith learning to use protein electrophoresis for studies in population genetics. This set the course of most of my research career.

What are your responsibilities?
1. Teaching (Evolution; Biology of Fishes, Amphibians, and Reptiles)
2. Advising graduate students
3. Maintaining extracurricular support for research
4. Curating the fish collection in the Oklahoma State University Collection of Vertebrates

What do you like best about your career?
1. The high degree of freedom to pursue academic interests relatively unencumbered by what others think I should be doing.
2. The opportunity to travel and muck around in the field under the guise of "all in a day's work."

What would be your most important piece of advice for someone considering the career you're in now?
Become an expert in natural history. Knowledge of natural history breeds questions.
