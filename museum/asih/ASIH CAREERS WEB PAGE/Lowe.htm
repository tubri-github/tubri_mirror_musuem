How did you first become interested in ichthyology?
I grew up on a small island off the New England coast. There wasn't anything else to do but fish, dive, fish… Plus, my Mom's family had been commercial fishers and whalers in New England since the 1600s, so I guess my heritage kind of steered my career path.

What steps did you have to take to get where you are today?
Worked my butt off! Not being the sharpest tack in the wall, I had to work harder than most to get where I am now. Probably the smartest thing I did was surround myself with the best and brightest. Working with people like Don Nelson, Dick Bray, Kim Holland, Tim Tricas and many other exceptional ichthyologists, really helped develop my abilities and approach to science.

Also, I think the side projects and diversions I entertained in my graduate career helped me get a tenure-track job faster. It might have taken me longer to get through grad school, but I was able to avoid post-docing by racking up some good pubs from side projects as they popped up. Again, working with colleagues like Brad Wetherbee, Carl Meyer, and Jerry Crow certainly help make the process fun and rewarding. As I look back, we certainly accomplished a lot as grad students.

What kinds of skills are required for your career? What experiences do you feel prepared you best for your job?
As an assistant professor at a major comprehensive University, the most valuable skill I need is the ability to "juggle!" In my faculty position, my time is supposed to be split into 50% teaching and 50% research. I consider myself very fortunate in that teaching comes somewhat easy to me, but nevertheless, most students have no idea exactly how much time goes into preparing lectures and laboratories. In addition, writing grants, establishing a research lab and coordinating students, collecting data and publishing are also incredibly time consuming. So, being able to "juggle" teaching commitments and maintaining an active research program is incredibly challenging. Being organized, a good communicator and leader are essential. I gained a lot of those skills in grad school working in great labs like Kim Holland's at the University of Hawaii. In his lab, we worked as a team and were given a lot of responsibility as graduate students. We were involved in every step of the scientific process, from grant preparation to writing up the manuscripts. It was excellent training for my current job. Learning how to balance those responsibilities and maintain my own research effort helped me hone multi-tasking abilities.

What are your responsibilities?
As mentioned above, my responsibilities are equally split between teaching and research. I teach between 1-3 classes per semester (both undergraduate and graduate) and serve on departmental, college, and University committees. I am expected to maintain an active research program and bring in grants. I am expected to mentor undergraduate and graduate students.

What do you like best about your career?
The diversity! Every day is an adventure and you never know what new opportunity will pop up. One of the unique aspects of academia is that you have the freedom to pursue so many different areas of research. Being able to try new things and learn new techniques, helps keep it fresh.

I also really enjoy my interactions with students. Although there are days when you want to set them adrift in a disabled boat, it's amazing to watch them develop.

What would be your most important piece of advice for someone considering the career you're in now?
Take advantage of all the opportunities you can as you're going through school. Volunteer to work in someone's lab, even if it's part time. Travel every opportunity you get! Learn to be a sponge - just "shut up" and absorb. Learn to ask "good" questions. Develop your writing and speaking skills. These are absolutely essential in my line of work. Make sure you REALLY love it (i.e., being a professor). If you don't, you will find that this job is way too much work for way too little money. But, if you really love it, none of that other stuff will really matter!
