Name: Brooks M. Burr
Current Position: Professor of Zoology, Southern Illinois University
Education: B.A., Greenville College, IL
M.S. & Ph.D., University of Illinois-Urbana, IL
Website: http://www.science.siu.edu/zoology/burr/index.html
Favorite Fish: Noturus flavater, Checkered Madtom
Favorite Ice Cream: Butter Pecan

 

How did you first become interested in ichthyology?
In college I worked one summer as a fisheries aid and made several collections of fishes in the Midwest. To have the fish identified correctly we took them to the Illinois Natural History Survey and there I met my future graduate advisor (Philip W. Smith) and one of my closest colleagues (Larry M. Page). I was very impressed by their ability to spot identify every fish we had collected.

What steps did you have to take to get where you are today?
After completing my doctoral studies and finding a tenure-track academic position I would say that learning how to obtain grant/contract funds to support graduate students and research was probably the biggest challenge and the biggest pressure outside of teaching and publishing research results.

What kinds of skills are required for your career?
What experiences do you feel prepared you best for your job? Various field skills related to different sampling designs for ecological and life history studies of freshwater fishes. Learning how to write a scientific paper. Gaining experience in communicating effectively in meetings where grant funds are at stake. Knowing the literature on North American fishes and knowing where the interesting problems are for future studies.

What are your responsibilities?
Teaching (about 50% time), research (about 35% time), Director of Undergraduate Studies (about 15% time). I have 11 graduate students working on a variety of problems that focus on the systematics or ecology of both North and South American fishes. I spend a fair amount of time talking with our 230 undergraduate majors trying to help them to see zoology as a valid career option.

What do you like best about your career?
I have enjoyed traveling and seeing fishes both here and in foreign countries; getting to know my ichthyological colleagues at other institutions; working with graduate students and enjoying their progress and success. I am still fascinated by the tremendous diversity of freshwater fishes and I know that I will continue to be interested in academic questions related to fish biology and systematics.


What would be your most important piece of advice for someone considering the career you're in now?
Get experience teaching the lecture and laboratory portions of freshman-level biology classes. Make contacts with personnel at agencies that deal with natural resources and find out what kind of research is needed. Go to the meetings where agency personnel discuss their upcoming projects. Collaborate with others in related fields to put together research proposals that are multifaceted. Talk with the program officers at NSF and find out what they are planning for in the future. Publish your work in journals with a wide audience. Go to professional society meetings and get to know your colleagues and their students.
