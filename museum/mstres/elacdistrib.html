Distribution of the Subgenus Elacatinus

The species of Elacatinus display some interesting distributional patterns when examined closely. The distributions below are based on Patrick Colin's dissertation work, which was published by T.F.H. in 1975. The outlines are rough boundaries. The species may not occur at all localities and islands within the boundary.

Fig. 1. Distribution of the E. oceanops group.

The first group to look at is the oceanops complex, consisting of oceanops, evelynae, illecebrosum and genie. Except for E. genie, the species have essentially non-overlapping boundaries. Bohlke & Robins (1968) and Colin (1975) suggest that oceanops, evelynae and illecebrosum are most closely related to each other. Elacatinus genie occurs entirely within the range of evelynae. Bohlke & Robins (1968) and Colin (1975) felt that genie, while placed with oceanops, is intermediate between the other oceanops members and prochilos and randalli. They based their hypothesis on dentition, the lack of a frenum, and the common habit of parasite cleaning and coral dwelling.

Fig. 2. Distribution of E. genie, E. prochilos and E. randalli.

When the distribution of genie is compared to prochilos and randalli, their ranges are allopatric. Sazima (1997) felt that figaro was closely related to prochilos and randalli, also because of parasite cleaning and coral dwelling. Elacatinus figaro is distributed in the south Atlantic along the coast of Brazil. If Sazima's hypothesized relationship holds true, then an interesting vicariance possibly exists. The discharge of the Amazon and Orinoco rivers may act as a barrier separating figaro from the rest of the elacatines.

Fig. 3. Distribution of E. horsti, E. chancei and E. tenox.

The remaining members of the Atlantic Elacatinus live in sponges. Three members of the horsti group (cf. Bohlke & Robins 1968) also exhibit a mostly allopatric distribution. Bohlke & Robins (1968) and Colin (1975) felt that these three species were closely allied. Elacatinus tenox has a wide disjunction. More collections along the coast and islands of northern Venezuela may ultimately reveal a wider distribution for tenox, or the two populations may be relicts of a once wider distribution.

Fig. 4. Distribution of E. atronasum, E. louisae and E. xanthiprora.

Colin (1975) thought that louisae and xanthiprora were closely related. Elacatinus atronasum is unique among Elacatinus because it spends much of its time swimming in the water column, only occasionally coming to rest on top of a coral head. This slight association with coral may reflect a common ancestry with other coral-dwelling elacatines or the habit may have evolved independently.
