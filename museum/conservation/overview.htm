EEOB 260 - Natural Resource Conservation in theory and practice

 

Course overview and objectives

 

In the United States, public interest in environmental protection and conservation of natural resources is entrusted to various agencies of state and federal government and an increasing number of non-governmental organizations (NGO's).  The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) is responsible for safeguarding air and water quality and protecting U.S. citizens from environmental hazards.  The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (FWS) is the principal federal agency protecting and enhancing fish wildlife, plants, and the habitats that support these organisms, for the continuing enjoyment of the American public.  Ideally, the activities of these agencies should be based on scientifically-established and ecologically-sound principles.  However, since these are governmental agencies, politics and bureaucratic "red tape" frequently interfere with best practices.  A growing number of NGO's (e.g., The Nature Conservancy, Sierra Club, Audubon Society) work in concert with governmental conservation agencies or serve as "watch-dogs" to ensure that the public trust is properly served.  What principles guide these organizations?

This course examines the theory and practice of natural resource preservation in the United States, and the wide spectrum of agencies and organizations involved in this endeavor.  Our focus will be on activities occurring within the State of Louisiana, especially the New Orleans vicinity.  We will begin with a discussion of basic principles of ecology and conservation biology.  This will be followed by an overview of the geology, climate, and ecoregions of Louisiana.  We will then examine land ownership in Louisiana and extents to which different habitats/ecoregions are protected.  This will be followed by an overview of the agencies involved in natural resource conservation and the environmental laws that guide activities of these agencies.  The remaining weeks of the course will be devoted to examining various conservation agencies and organizations operating in Louisiana, the parks and preserves they manage, and how well these preserves address conservation needs in the state.  These discussions will be informed by representatives of the agencies themselves.  

  

Requirements:  The class will meet three hours per week: two hours of lecture; one hour of reflection.  Students are required to complete a minimum of 40 hours of service over the course of the semester with a local natural resource agency/organization.   Students will keep a journal to document service activities.  Journals will be collected and graded at the end of the semester. Students will take two exams (mid-semester and final) based on information presented in lecture. Lastly, students will work in groups on assigned research projects and will give presentations based on their research.
