Background:


Under the direction of the Department of the Interior, the National Park Service’s (NPS) was established in 1916 with the mission "...to promote and regulate the use of the...national parks...which purpose is to conserve the scenery and the natural and historic objects and the wild life therein and to provide for the enjoyment of the same in such manner and by such means as will leave them unimpaired for the enjoyment of future generations."  As landscapes continue to change in dramatic ways, it becomes even more pressing to preserve natural and historical resources for the benefit of future generations.

Jean Lafitte National Historical Park and Preserve (JELA) was established in 1978 in order to “…preserve for the education, inspiration, and benefit of present and future generations significant examples of natural and historical resources of the Mississippi Delta region and to provide for their interpretation in such manner as to portray the development of cultural diversity in the region.”    With the headquarters located in the French Quarter, JELA is comprised of several park units including Crescent City (Chalmette Battlefield, Chalmette Cemetery, French Quarter Site), Acadian (with locations in Eunice, Thibodaux, and Lafayette), and the Barataria Preserve.

The Barataria Preserve is located 15 miles south of New Orleans along the eastern shore of Lake Salvador and is part of the ecologically significant Barataria Estuary.  The 18,500-acre Preserve consists of three broad habitat types: bottomland hardwood forest, swamp, and marsh.  The marsh at the Preserve is one of the largest portions of floating marsh in the world.  The Preserve supports an abundance of wildlife and plant communities, including neotropical migrant songbirds, furbears, reptiles and amphibians, and commercially and recreationally important fin and shellfish.

Projects:

The following projects are primarily data entry and analysis with some field work included.  Project supervision will depend on the individual student’s skills and ability to work independently.  Depending on student’s schedules, data entry can be completed independently and field work will be conducted according to researcher’s schedule.  Although the main office for JELA is located in the French Quarter, field work will take place at the Barataria Preserve on the Westbank.

Analyze Historic and Current Nutria Harvest Data and Assist with Radio Telemetry Research Project

The Barataria Preserve is plagued with non-native nutria which have historically caused eat-outs and destroyed marsh vegetation.  The Preserve has allowed nutria trapping since the early 1980’s and currently has five trappers actively trying to reduce the nutria population.  In order to accurately assess the number of nutria harvested on the Preserve and compare harvests among years, the number of acres trapped and effort needs to be taken into consideration.  Students will input data into a database and make yearly comparisons.

In addition, students will have the opportunity to assist Lauren Nolfo (PhD candidate, Tulane University) with her research project examining the seasonal movements of nutria in the Preserve.  Ms. Nolfo is using radio transmitters to determine movements and habitat associations.  Field work will be coordinated with Ms. Nolfo and according to the student’s schedule.  This will be a valuable opportunity to learn about radio telemetry, marsh vegetation, and nutria behavior.

 

Compile White-tailed Deer Harvest Data and Assist with an Ongoing Research Project

The Barataria Preserve allows hunting of white-tailed deer during the annual state season.  Harvest numbers and hunter effort fluctuate among years.  Students will compile data from previous years and input it into a database.  Information about the number of deer killed, age, and sex needs to be analyzed to evaluate harvest activities and the health of the herd. 

Students will also have the opportunity to participate in an ongoing LSU research project.  Researchers from Louisiana State University have been working on a project to quantify the population of white-tailed deer on the Preserve and develop harvest recommendations.  In order to assess deer herbivory and forest regeneration, exclosures were established and seedlings were planted inside.  Students will assist researchers in measuring the height of planted seedlings and removing them from the exclosure, and constructing/repairing exclosures, as needed. 

 

Input Breeding Bird Survey Data into Database for Analysis

Breeding bird surveys have been conducted on the Barataria Preserve.  Much of this data is unable to be analyzed because it is in the form of raw data sheets and not in a database.  This information is of interest to the Park to determine trends in species diversity and abundance.  Students will be required to enter data into a database and conduct a literature search of avian research conducted in the Preserve and at areas with similar habitats.

The Nature Conservancy, Louisiana Chapter

Together with its members and conservation partners, the Louisiana Chapter of The Nature Conservancy  protects over 325,000 acres of critical natural lands.  The Louisiana Chapter of The Nature Conservancy  manages several preserves and projects in the Greater New Orleans Area. The three projects outlined below as service learning options all involve research that students can do primarily from campus.  However, students will have the opportunity to visit preserves at least once during the semester, to do various kinds of volunteer work on premises.

Web site: http://nature.org/wherewework/northamerica/states/louisiana/

"Nelwyn McInnis" nmcinnis@tnc.org

Projects:    

1)  Forest Legacy Program - this is a new federal program through the US Forest Service.  Each state must apply for the program.  This year TNC is working with the state Office of Forestry to complete the application.  The State of Louisiana is contracting with TNC to complete an "Assessment of need".  TNC is looking for students to do research and find examples of similar assessments from other states, which TNC can use as models, and general information that should go into these reports. 

2) Research on habitat and ecological needs of species at risk in the Pearl River and major tributaries.   TNC is working with state and federal agencies to conserve the unique biodiversity in the Pearl River system and to learn more about the ecology of its most vulnerable species.  Information is needed on the habitat and other ecological needs of rare or declining fishes (e.g., Gulf sturgeon), turtles (e.g., ring sawback turtle) and mussels in the Pearl River system. 

3) Restoration of sand and gravel mines in the southeast.  Sedimentation resulting from sand and gravel mining is a major factor contributing to degradation of the Pearl River.  TNC is working with the aggregate industry to promote Best Management Practices (BMP's) in sand and gravel mining that ensure stream quality.  TNC has a preserve on the Pearl River in Mississippi called Mike's Island that has some old gravel mine sites that are in need restoration.  Students would do research into BMP’s for sand and gravel mining.
