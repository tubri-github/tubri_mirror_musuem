Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

 

Noturus munitus, Suttkus and Taylor, 1965

Frecklebelly madtom

 

www.flmnh.ufl.edu/.../ frecklebellymadtom.htm- Photo by B. H. Bauer, Florida Museum of Natural History

 

Synonymy:

None

 

Conservation Status:

Population is spotty throughout much of its distribution.  Habitat loss due to dredging, sedimentation, and impoundments are the main factors threatening this species.  A significant decline has occurred in the Mobile Bay drainage and agencies currently need more precise information concerning the environmental limits of the species.

 

Federal T&E Status: Lower Risk/near threatened (NT)

State Ranking: S2S3

NatureServe Global Ranking: G3

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution:  North America; Mississippi, Louisiana, Georgia, Alabama, and Tennessee. Occur in the Pearl, Mississippi, Tombigebee, Etowah, Cahaba, Sipsey, Conasauga River systems.

 

Habitat: Freshwater; occur in rocky riffles and runs of medium to large rivers systems with moderate to high flow. Require low siltation and are often found near vegetation.

 

Biology:

Feeding: Noturus munitus  is a benthic species, that demonstrates nocturnal behavior. They feed on aquatic insect larvae.

 

Reproduction: Spawn during the summer months, and are nest guarders.

 

Morphology:

Body Shape: The Frecklebelly Madtom has a stout body with the deepest point located beneath or in front of the dorsal fin.

         Maximum size: 9.5 cm TL (male/unsexed)    

 

Life Colors:  The body is yellow to dark brown with dark mottling above and 4 dark saddles. There are large light spots in front of the dorsal fin often incompletely enclosed by dark pigment. The belly usually has dark specks. The fins are mottled or blotched and the dorsal fin has a broad dark band near the clear edge. The caudal fin has 2 dark crescent-shaped bands; 1 near the middle and 1 near the clear edge. The adipose fin has a dark brown or black band extending to the edge.

 

External Morphology: The pectoral spine is large with 5-10 large teeth on the rear edge and large teeth on the front edge. The caudal fin is straight or slightly rounded and nearly free from the adipose fin.         

 

Morphologically similar species: The Northern Madtom, Noturus stigmosus, lacks brown specks on the belly, has a dark band confined to the lower half of the adipose fin, 2 light spots in front of the dorsal fin enclosed by darker pigment, and a dark crescent-shaped band in the middle of the caudal fin that extends forward across the upper and lower caudal rays to the caudal peduncle. The Piebald Madtom, N. gladiator, lacks brown specks on the belly; has a bolder pattern, a dark bar in the adipose fin connected to the dark bar in the anal fin, a dark crescent-shaped band in the middle of the caudal fin joined to the dark pigment on the caudal peduncle forming 2 distinct yellow spots at the base of the fin. The Neosho Madtom, Noturus placidus, lacks prominent saddles and dark specks on the belly, has small teeth on front of the pectoral spine, white lower front caudal rays, and a dusky band confined to the lower half of the adipose fin. (Page, 2007)
