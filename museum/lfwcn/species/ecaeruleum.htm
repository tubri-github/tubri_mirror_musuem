Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

Etheostoma caeruleum (Storer, 1845)

Rainbow Darter

                                                                                         

 

 

Conservation Status: Louisiana Animal of Conservation Concern

 

Federal T&E Status: None

State Ranking: S2S3

NatureServe Global Ranking: G5

 

Habitat and Biology

 

Distribution: North America: Great Lakes and Mississippi River basins from southern Ontario in Canada and from western New York to Minnesota, and south to northern Alabama and Arkansas in the USA. Isolated populations in southwestern Mississippi and eastern Louisiana, and in upper Potomac River drainage in West Virginia, USA.

 

Habitat:  Moderately swift (60 cm/s) runs and riffles over firm gravel or pebble substrata. Except during cold temperatures, rainbow darters tend to remain in the same type of habitat throughout the year, with a tendency to move into regions with large rocks after the reproductive season. Rainbow darters show a wide range of temperature tolerance, with average winter and summer critical maximum temperatures of 25.4°C and 36.4°C respectively.

Climate/Range: Temperate; 46°N - 30°N

 

Biology:

            Feeding:  Rainbow darters forage over the surface of the substratum rather than moving into the interstices (Schlosser and Toth 1984, Ross et al. 2001). Diet consists of, midge, black fly, and mayfly larvae; diet tends to favor may and black fly larvae as size increases.  There is evidence of a shift in prey type preference from fall to the winter (Ross et al. 2001).

           

            Reproduction:  Spawning occurs in the spring over riffles with gravel and/ or rubble. Males occupy riffles, while females tend to congregate below riffles. Males may defend breeding territories, 12-30cm in diameter. When a female swims into a riffle she is met by one or more males. Larger males may scare smaller males away. Prior to spawning the female enters the gravel headfirst, positioning herself almost vertically, she then moves down and forward so that her head is exposed but the back half of her body remains buried. The male then approaches the female curving his body around hers so that his tail is partially buried. The couple then quivers fertilizing and burying the eggs at the same time. The female may then swim further up the rifle spawning with the same or different males (Ross et al. 2001).

 

 

Morphology:

            Body Shape:  A deep bodied, darter. Most adults are less than 50mm SL

            Max. size: 65mm SL

           

            Life Colors: Back is greenish brown to tan, with 6-10 darker dorsal saddles; the first two to four of the saddles, are wide and intense. There are 10-13 vertical bars on the sides that are most distinct below the lateral line, which is incomplete. The undersides of the head and belly are silvery white. In breeding males, the sides have rows of reddish spots, and the caudal peduncle is often reddish orange with two orange spots at the caudal base; the margin of the first dorsal fin is blue, with a narrow yellow-white middle band followed by a broad red-orange basal band; the second dorsal also has a blue marginal band, with a thick red-orange middle band, followed by a blue band at the base (Ross et al. 2001). All other fins of breeding males show some combination of blue and red-orange coloration.

 

            External Morphology: Subterminal mouth, with a broad premaxillary frenum; the belly and opercle are scaled, the breast is usually unscaled, and the cheek is either unscaled or with embedded scales along the margin of the orbit.

 

Morphologically similar species: Male darters in breeding colors are distinctive in their range, however females, juveniles, and preserved specimens may be difficult to distinguish from other members of the subgenus Oligocephalus. Yet the rainbow darter can be distinguished from most members of Oligocephalus because the first two to four dorsal saddles are much wider and darker compared to the other saddles.
