Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

 

Phenacobius mirabilis, (Girard, 1856)

Suckermouth minnow

 

picture (Phmir_ui.jpg) by The Native Fish Conservancy

 

Synonymy:

Etheostoma clarum, (Jordan & Meek, 1885)

 

Conservation Status:

Reports indicate that the Western Sand Darter is declining in abundance across its distribution. 

Several states such as Iowa have elevated its status to threatened.

Further monitoring is suggested.

 

Federal T&E Status: Not evaluated

State Ranking: S2

NatureServe Global Ranking: G3

 

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution: Found in the North American river basins of the Mississippi and a limited number of Gulf Coast drainages (i.e. Sabine, Galveston Bay, Colorado River, and upper Pecos River).  Also, distributed throughout the western Lake Erie watershed in Ohio.

 

Habitat: Freshwater; gravel to rocky sediment in turbid to clear, medium to small streams and rivers.

 

Biology:

         Feeding: Invertivore; benthic; grazer (Goldstein and Simon 1999). Feeds by probing the substrate with its sensitive snout and lips (Starrett 1950a).

 

         Reproduction: The Suckermouth minnow spawns in late spring or early summer (Hubbs and Ortenburger 1929; Starrett 1951; Pflieger 1997); late May to mid-July depending on area (Carlander 1969). Thought to spawn in gravelly riffles (Becker 1983)

 

Morphology:

Body Shape:  Elongate, cylindrical; short, broadly rounded head; small mouth and eyes.

         Maximum size: 12 cm TL (male/unsexed)

 

Life Colors: Back and upper sides silvery green with dark lateral stripe ending in a spot at base of caudal fin; abdomen whitish; peritoneum silvery with black spots. Rays of dorsal, caudal, and pectoral fins lightly outlined with melanophores (Sublette et al. 1990).

 

External Morphology: Lower lip thick with fleshy lobe on each side that is partially separated from mandible by a groove (best observed from the front); lateral line usually not decurved, either straight or with a broad arch; premaxillaries protractile; upper lip separated from skin of snout by a deep groove continuous across the midline. (Hubbs et al., 1991) Posterior scales of belly naked; lateral line complete (Ross 2001)

 

Morphologically similar species: Differs from chubs (Macrhybopsis) in always lacking barbels, and from Pimephales vigilax, the bullhead minnow, in mouth position (strongly inferior versus nearly terminal; Ross 2001). Phenacobius mirabilis can be distinguished from any small minnow or sucker by the fleshy posterior lower lip characteristic of the genus (Boschung and Mayden 2004).
