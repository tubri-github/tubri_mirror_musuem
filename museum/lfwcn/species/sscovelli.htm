Syngnathus scovelli, (Evermann & Kendall, 1896)

Gulf pipefish

 

Photo by Sneegas, G. W

 

Synonymy:

Siphostoma scovelli, Evermann & Kendall, 1896

 

Conservation Status:

Louisiana Species of Conservation concern.

 

Federal T&E Status: Not evaluated

State Ranking: S4

NatureServe Global Ranking: G5

 

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution:  Western Atlantic Ocean; range extends from Florida and Gulf of Mexico south to Central and South America and the Caribbean.

 

Habitat: Freshwater; brackish; marine. Usually found in shallow shoreline areas in bays and estuaries.  Enter and reproduce in freshwater occasionally. Prefer euryhaline environments with submergent vegetation such as seagrass. beds

 

Biology:feeds on

         Feeding: Feeding take place during the day, with most of the diet made up of small crustaceans: copepods, amphipods, tanaids, and isopods. (Brook 1977)

 

         Reproduction: In most of Syngnathus scovelli’s range, spawning can occur throughout the year (Hellier 1967; Begovac and Wallace 1987), though along the northern Gulf Coast, there is a winter decline in reproductive activity (Joseph 1957). Ovoviviparous; males carry eggs in brood pouch under tail. The male brood pouch forms at maturity and then remains developed for the remainder of the male's life (Ross 2001). Like other syngnathids, gulf pipefish have an elaborate courtship ritual, or "liebenspiel," which is initiated by the female. Display consists of the pair swimming vertically and bobbing to water surface, then intertwining of the bodies, and the male rubbing his brood pouch along the bottom (Joseph 1957).

 

Morphology:

Body Shape: The Gulf pipefish is small with an elongate body. (Ross 2001)

Maximum size: 18.3 cm TL (male/unsexed)                       

 

Life Colors:  The body is uniformly brown or dark olive green, with vertical parallel silvery white bars along the sides. The bars may appear Y-shaped, and become wider and more bandlike along the tail. The underside of the snout may be unpigmented, and the snout may have an unpigmented stream middorsally. The rays of the anal and pectoral fins are outlined with melanophores. The dorsal and caudal fins are more densely pigmented (Ross 2001).

 

External Morphology: The body is encased in bony rings and has a rounded caudal fin. The snout is short, the pelvic fins are absent, and the anal fin is greatly reduced. (Ross 2001).

 

Morphologically similar species: The gulf pipefish is most similar to the chain pipefish. It differs from it in having 16-17 (versus 19-20) trunk rings, 31 (versus 35-36) tail rings, and in having a shorter snout. The pipefishes can be distinguished from all other freshwater fishes by their lack of pelvic fins and by having their body encased in bony rings (Ross 2001).
