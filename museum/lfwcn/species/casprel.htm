Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

Crystallaria asprella (Jordan, 1878)

Crystal Darter

 

 

Synonymy: Ammocrypta asprella (Jordan, 1878)

 

                                                                                           

 

 

Conservation Status: Louisiana Animal of Conservation Concern; This species is vulnerable because it has a discontinuous range in the Mississippi River basin and a portion of the Gulf Slope; less than 100 occurrences; rare throughout range; declining in many areas; development near rivers and continued habitat alteration pose continued threats.

 

Federal T&E Status: NA

State Ranking: S2S3

NatureServe Global Ranking: G3

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution: It once occurred in large tributaries of the Missouri, Ohio, and Mississippi Rivers, ranging from Wisconsin and Minnesota east to Ohio and West Virginia and south to Mississippi and Louisiana. Unfortunately, this species seems to be very sensitive to habitat alteration and has been eliminated from much of its former range (Ross et al. 2001). In Louisiana it can now only be found in the northern part of the state (Naturserve 2009).

 

Habitat:  The crystal darter inhabits clear to slightly turbid water of raceways and swift to moderately swift riffles of small to medium rivers with expanses of clean sand or gravel; it does not associate with mud, clay, or submerged vegetation (Etnier and Starnes 1993, Pflieger 1997, Ross 2001, Boschung and Mayden 2004). Usually it occurs in water more than 60 centimeters deep with strong current.

Climate/Range: Temperate; 45°N - 31°N

 

Biology:

            Feeding: The crystal darter buries itself in with only the eyes protruding as it lies in wait for passing prey (R. J. Miller and Robison 1973). Prey consists of various aquatic insects including midges, black flies, mayflies, crane flies, and caddisflies (Ross et al. 2001)

           

            Reproduction: Spawning likely occurs in early spring, based on the development of breeding tubercles in males (Collette 1965) and on the January-April spawning season in Arkansas (Ross et al. 2001).

 

Morphology:

            Body Shape:  A large translucent darter with four distinct wide dorsal saddles; the head is conical and the top of the head is broad and flat with a narrow interorbital region. Caudal peduncle is long and narrow. It appears that fish in southern populations seem to be smaller (Ross et al. 2001).

            Max. size: 144mm SL

           

            Life Colors: The back is a light olive green with four wide saddles that extend ventrally to an iridescent blue mid-lateral stripe, which is interrupted by elongate brown blotches. The undersides of the head and body are silvery white. The preorbital bar is well developed and continuous and has a wide band of pigment on the snout and upper jaw (Ross et al. 2001).

 

            External Morphology: The crystal darter has a forked caudal fin; large oval eyes; a horizontal mouth; and a well developed premaxillary frenum. An opercular spine is present, but is included in the fleshy margin of the opercle. The nape is scaled, the cheek and opercle are partly scaled, and the breast and belly are unscaled. The lateral line is complete. If present, there are one to four pored scales extending onto the caudal fin.

 

Morphologically similar species: The crystal darter most resembles species of the genus Ammocrypta, except that the crystal darter has four wide dorsal saddles, up to four pored scales on the caudal fin, 11 or more anal fins, 12 or more dorsal spines, and 79 or more lateral scales (Ross et al. 2001).
