Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

 

Alosa alabamae, Jordan & Evermann, 1896

Suckermouth minnow

 

picture (Alala_ui.jpg) by The Native Fish Conservancy

 

Synonymy:

Elosa ohiensis, Evermann, 1902

   Meletta suoerii, Valenciennes, 1874

 

Conservation Status:

Impoundments, sedmentation, and contaminates have contributed significantly to the limited distribution of the Suckermouth minnow. 

Agencies are focusing on removing migratory blocks to help the recovery of the species.

 

Federal T&E Status: Endangered (EN)

State Ranking: S1

NatureServe Global Ranking: G3

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution: Western Central Atlantic; Gulf of Mexico from the Mississippi delta east to Choctawatchee River.  Also, occurs in Louisiana, Mississippi, Iowa, Arkansas, and West Virginia river drainages.

 

Habitat: Freshwater; brackish; marine pelagic-neritic zone/near shore.

 

Biology:

            Feeding: Adult Alabama shad apparently feed little or not at all while in fresh water, as

evidenced by the lack of food in their stomachs when captured. Juvenile Alabama shad in the Apalachicola and other river drainages feed largely on

aquatic dipterans and small fishes. The feeding habits of Alabama shad at sea are unknown.

           

            Reproduction: The Alabama shad is an anadromous species, entering freshwater rivers to

spawn and then returning to the sea to mature. Adults ascend open waters of medium to

large Gulf Coast rivers from January to July. Alabama shad spawn over sand and gravel substrates in reaches with moderate

current velocities. Juveniles are found in stream margin habitats over sand and gravel and then apparently move into salt water habitats at the end of their first summer. Adults may live to 6 years of age, and are usually 2 years old at time of spawning. Adults may spawn more than one time, although the proportion that repeat spawn is probably low.

 

Morphology:           

            Body Shape: Alabama shad has an elongated,laterally compressed body with a forked tail. Adult females are larger than males and

may grow to 500 mm total length (ca. 20 in).

                        Maximum size: 51 cm TL (male/unsexed)              

         

            Life Colors:  The overall body coloration is silvery with a blue-green back; a darker shoulder

spot may be evident in some individuals.

        

           

            External Morphology: This species has a large terminal mouth with a lower jaw projecting only slightly beyond the upper jaw. There is a sharply angled notch in the center of the upper jaw.

 

Morphologically similar species: The Alabama shad is distinguished from American shad by having 42–48 (vs. 59–73) gill rakers on the lower limb of the first gill arch, non-glossy cheek and opercular bones covered with conspicuous mucous pores and canals (vs. glossy cheek and opercular bones with inconspicuous mucous pores and canals), and a pelvic fin axillary scale which is approximately 75% of the fin length vs. ~50% of the fin length. These species are also allopatric, with Alabama shad inhabiting the Gulf coast of Florida west to the Mississippi River, whereas American shad range from the Atlantic coast of northeastern Florida north to Maine and southeastern Canada. The Alabama shad is distinguished from the skipjack herring (A. chrysochloris) by having a sharply angled notch in the upper jaw (vs. a weakly angled or obtuse notch), a slightly protruding lower jaw (vs. a strongly protruding lower jaw), and more than 30 gill rakers on the lower limb of the first gill arch (vs. less than 30, usually 18-24). It differs from the gizzard shad and threadfin shad in lacking an elongated last dorsal fin ray and having scales along the midline in front of the dorsal fin.
