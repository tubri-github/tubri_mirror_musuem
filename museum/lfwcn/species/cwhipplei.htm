Cyprinella whipplei, Girard, 1856

Habitat and Biology

Distribution:  North American drainages; Black Warrior River in Alabama, and the Mississippi River basin.

 

Habitat: Freshwater; sandy/rocky bottoms in riffles of small to medium streams that are moderately clear.  Less common in pools.

 

Biology:

Feeding: C. whipplei is a primarily insectivorous species, feeding in the water column, yet they may shift to benthic feeding during colder weather.

 

Reproduction: Spawns near cover or obstructions and attaches eggs to the underside of rocks or vegetation. The steelcolor spawns in crevices, usually in areas with at least some flow, and in the middle to upper water column. Males are territorial, defending a part of a suitable crevice from other males.  A female who is ready to spawn moves to the spawning crevice and may be approached by a number of males, resulting in intense male-male competition. The victorious male takes a position slightly above the female, with his head about even with her pectoral fins. (Ross, 2001)

 

Morphology:

Body Shape:  The Steelcolor shiner is laterally compressed with a small subconical head. The dorsal and ventral profiles of the body are about equally curved, and the greatest body depth is just in front of the dorsal fin origin.

 

Maximum size: 16 cm TL (male/unsexed)              

 

Life Colors: The back and sides of the steelcolor shiner are blue to olive green becoming silvery on the flanks and silvery white on the undersides.  Dorsal and lateral scales are strongly outlined with melanophores, creating a cross-hatched pattern.

 

External Morphology: C. whipplei has a terminal to slightly subterminal mouth, small eyes, and usually nine anal rays. The dorsal fin origin is above or just posterior to the pelvic fin origin.

 

Morphologically similar species:

The steelcolor shiner is most similar to the bluntface, spotfin, and whitetail shiners. It differs from the spotfin in having nine rather than eight anal rays. And in having anterior dorsal fin membranes pigmented in non breeding adults. The steelcolor differs from the whitetail shiner be lacking the white triangles on the caudal fin that characterizes the fish. The only reliable way to differentiate between the bluntface and the steelcolor shiners is to observe caudal pigmentation, and the pattern of breeding tubercles, because the steelcolor has a distinct hiatus between the snout and interorbital tubercles. (Ross, 2001)
