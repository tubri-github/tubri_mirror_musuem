Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

Acipenser oxyrinchus desotoi (Vladykov, 1955)

Gulf Sturgeon

 

 

Synonymy: Acipenser oxyrhynchus desotoi Vladykov, 1955

 

Conservation Status:

Louisiana Animal of Conservation Concern; Imperiled because the historical breeding range in rivers feeding the northern Gulf of Mexico has been greatly reduced by dams and overfishing; incidental take in fisheries may be significant; breeding range reduced to seven river systems.

 

Federal T&E Status: Threatened

State Ranking: S1S2

NatureServe Global Ranking: G3T2

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution: Occurs in Gulf of Mexico drainages from Tampa bay to the Mississippi River. Its present range is primarily from the Suwannee River in Florida to eastern Louisiana (Wooley 1985, Ross et al. 2001)

 

Habitat:  Primarily found in marine/estuarine habitats, until it moves into large rivers that drain into the Gulf of Mexico to spawn.

Climate/Range: Subtropical; 32°N - 28°N

 

Biology:

            Feeding: Sturgeon feed by using their sharp snouts to root along the bottom, where they suck prey in with their protrusile mouth. C.R. Gilbert (1989) suggests that the barbels in front of their mouth are highly sensitive and used to help detect prey, which are generally soft bodied. Common foods during their marine period include lancelets, polychaete worms, gastropods, shrimp, amphipods, and isopods. Items eaten in freshwater include aquatic insects, including mayflies and caddisflies, worms, and bivalve mollusks (Huff 1975; Mason and Clugston 1993; Moser and Ross 1995; Ross et al. 2001)

           

            Reproduction: A. o. desotoi is an anadromous species, adults moving into the rivers in the spring for spawning and moving back out to sea in the fall. They start entering the fresh water from mid-February through May. Most movement into rivers occurs at water temperatures of 16-25ºC (Ross et al. 2001). Upstream movement, once in freshwater, may go through June (A.M. Foster and Clugston 1997). Spawning habitat is made of running water, over rubble or cobble areas, or below waterfalls. Young sturgeon may spend up to six years in fresh water before moving out to sea, yet the time spent in freshwater for the gulf sturgeon may be significantly shorter, possibly less than one year (Huff 1975; Ross et al. 2001). There is evidence that the gulf sturgeon returns to the same river system every year.

 

 

Morphology:

Body Shape:  The gulf sturgeon is a large, primitive, relatively stocky, cylindrical fish, with an elongated and wedge-shaped head. They can reach very large sizes generally reaching 1.5 - 2.2 m, with females tending to be larger and longer lived than males (Ross et al. 2001)

            Max. size: 2.1m with historical records of specimens reaching 2.8m TL. Alexander (1905) mentioned an account of a 4.3m TL

           

Life Colors:  The back and sides are grey-brown to bluish black, fading to white on the belly. The dorsal scutes have a white spot on the keel and point, which contrasts with the darker background color (Ross et al. 2001)

 

External Morphology: The gulf sturgeon has four fleshy barbels in front of the mouth, which are positioned on the underside of the head, five rows of bony plates on each side with a prominent dorsal ridge; the tail fin is 2-lobed, with upper lobe longest; there is also a Spiracle above each eye.

Morphologically similar species: Acipenser o. desotoi can be differentiated from its northern subspecies (Acipenser o. oxyrinchus) by its much longer spleen, which extends posteriorly beyond the middle loop of the small intestine, and by its larger head. The gulf sturgeon can be distinguished from the lake sturgeon by the whitish viscera, a lower gill raker number (17-27) and by the paired postdorsal and preanal shields (Ross et al. 2001).
