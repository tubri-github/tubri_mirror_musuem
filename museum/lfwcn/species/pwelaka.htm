Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

Pteronotropis welaka (Evermann and Kendall, 1898)

Bluenose shiner

                                                                                        

 

Synonymy: Notropis welaka Evermann and Kendall, 1898

 

Conservation Status: Louisiana Animal of Conservation

Concern

Federal T&E Status: None

State Ranking: S1S2

NatureServe Global Ranking: G3G4

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution: North America: St. Johns River drainage, Florida; in the Gulf Coast drainages (mostly below Fall Line) from Apalachicola River, Georgia and Florida to Pearl River, Mississippi and Louisiana, US

 

Habitat: Benthopelagic; freshwater; Quiet backwaters and pools of blackwater streams and small to medium rivers and spring runs; usually with thick vegetation nearby. Occurs in both low-pH and high-pH environments. Usually found schooling in fairly deep water (of 1-2 m depth); Limited information on food indicates a largely herbivorous diet.

 Climate/Range: Subtropical; 34°N - 28°N

 

Biology:

            Feeding:  There is limited information on the feeding habits of P. welaka, but what is known seems to indicate a herbivorous diet.

            Reproduction: Spawning season runs from Mat to August, with the peak being from May to July. During the spawning season adults are more common in unvegetated areas and are often associated with longear sunfish (Lepomis megalotis) nests.(Johnston and Knight 1999)

 

Morphology:

            Body Shape:  A small shiner measuring 1.3 - 1.9 in. (33 - 48 mm); a slender minnow with a clylindrical, slightly compressed body; a pointed snout with a terminal to subterminal snout, slightly oblique mouth; eye diameter goes into snout length 0.7-0.9 times; Body depth goes into SL 4.2-5.3 times (Ross et al. 2001). Max. size: 42 mm SL (50 mm TL)

           

            Life Colors: olive colored with a dark lateral stripe bordered above by a narrow amber stripe, a dark caudal spot highlighted by light-colored areas above and below, and a blue “nose” (adults only). Adult males have large, darkly pigmented dorsal fins and yellow pelvic and anal fins streaked with black (Ross et al. 2001).

 

            External Morphology: Broad lateral band; incomplete lateral line; There are 15-17 predorsal, diagonal scale rows; the breast and belly are fully scaled. The insertion of the dorsal fin is posterior to the pelvic fin base, but the dorsal origin is equidistant between the snout and caudal fin base; predorsal length goes into goes into SL 1.8-2.0 times. Usually 3-13 pored scales in the lateral line.

 

Morphologically similar species: Adults are unmistakable. Young are similar to several small shiners with a dark lateral band: the iron color shiner (Notropis chalybaeus) has an intensely pigmented mouth interior; the coastal shiner (Notropis petersoni) has a much smaller caudal spot; the weed shiner (Notropis texanus) also has a small caudal spot. These species tend to have the dorsal fin positioned above (equal to) the pelvic fins versus posteriorly in the bluenose shiner. Also Juveniles are similar to the Flagfin shiner (Pteronotropis signipinnis) which has a complete lateral line, larger lateral band, a less cylindrical body shape and live Flagfin have a yellow-orange caudal pigmentation (Georgia DNR 1999, Ross et al. 2001)
