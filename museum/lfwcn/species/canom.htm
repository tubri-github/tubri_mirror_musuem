Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

Campostoma anomalum (Rafinesque, 1820)

Central Stoneroller

 

                                                                                        

 

 

Conservation Status: Louisiana Animal of Conservation Concern

 

Federal T&E Status: NA

State Ranking: S2

NatureServe Global Ranking: G5

 

Habitat and Biology

 

Distribution: C.anomalum is widespread throughout the central and eastern United States, except for the Atlantic coastal drainages (Burr 1980b), Burr and Cashner(1983) reported  that the southern limit to their distribution was Tunica bayou in Louisiana and Mississippi.

 

Habitat: Common in small to medium sized streams, where it often occurs in large schools. It generally occupies small riffles and quiet water habitats (Burr and Smith 1976; W.J. Matthews, 1990; Ross et al. 2001). Newly hatched fish school and feed near vegetated stream margins and in quiet warm backwaters in the late spring and early summer; juveniles frequently inhabit swifter waters around vegetated riffles during the summer and fall, and are commonly found in midstream habitats in pools having algal mats and moderately flowing currents (Theodorakis 1987; Leonard and Orth 1988; Mundahl and Ingersoll 1989; Edwards 1997).

Climate/Range: Subtropical; 48°N - 26°N

 

Biology:

            Feeding: Primarily herbivorous, using its cartilaginous lower jaw to feed on filamentous algae, diatoms, aquatic insects (Fowler and Tabor 1985, McNeely 1987). Young fish feed on rotifers, filamentous algae, and microcrustacea (Edwards 1997). In pools of small streams, grazing pressure from the central stoneroller results in very limited amounts of filamentous algae, with most f the stream bottom stripped of algae. Because the dense algal mats also support many kinds of aquatic insects, grazing by the stonerollers has a decided effect on the other kinds of organisms found in the stream (Ross et al. 2001)

           

            Reproduction: Spawning occurs from April to June, during this time the male stonerollers will build a nest of pebbles. Most nests are around 15-30cm in diameter, and generally 7.6 cm in depth. Nesting habitats generally are in shallow, flowing areas near a deep pool that serves as a refuge from predators. During nest construction, the male picks up a pebble in his mouth and moves from the center to the edge of the nest pit (R. J. Miller1962a, Ross et al. 2001). The nest pits are actively defended and maintained, primarily by males larger than 80mm, yet males don’t stay in a given pit for more than 10-15 minutes (Ross et al. 2001).

 

Morphology:

            Body Shape:  The central stoneroller is a stout minnow with a body that is oval to round in cross-section; with a blunt snout (Ross et al. 2001).

            Max. size: 118mm SL; Some northern populations grow larger  with a TL of 287mm (Lennon and Parker 1960)

           

            Life Colors: Dark olivaceous color on the back, grading to whitish on the underside; sides of most adults are marked by randomly scattered, small, dark spots which represent regenerated scales; fins are colorless (Miller and Robison 2004). Breeding males develop striking color patterns, the entire dorsum becoming dark slate-gray; undersides of body and lower fins are pale, sometimes yellowish; dorsal, anal, and pelvic fins are banded with black pigment (Miller 1962).

 

            External Morphology: The mouth is inferior and overhung by a fleshy snout; the lower jaw terminates in a distinctive cartilaginous shelf; barbells are not present on the central stoneroller. The dorsal fin origin is above or slightly behind the pelvic fin origin. Mature males have large, slightly hooked breeding tubercles on the top of the head between the nape and the nostrils (R. J. Miller1962a).

 

Morphologically similar species: The species the central stoneroller most closely resembles is the largescale stoneroller (Campostoma oligolepis), although their ranges do not overlap. The difference between the two species is more noticeable in breeding males. The central stoneroller males when in breeding condition have a well-developed black band near the base of the anal fin, and a crescent shaped row of tubercles along the inner margin of each nostril. Other than the breeding males, these two species are difficult to distinguish one species from the other (Ross et al. 2001).
