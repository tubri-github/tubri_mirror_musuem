Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

Percina lenticula (Richards and Knapp, 1964)

Freckled Darter

 

 

                                                                                             

 

 

Conservation Status:

Louisiana Animal of Conservation Concern; P. lenticula utilizes a small range in streams in the southeastern U.S, also there are usually few individuals present in a population, thus the species is currently imperiled.

 

Federal T&E Status: None

State Ranking: S1

NatureServe Global Ranking: G2

 

Habitat and Biology

 

Distribution: Restricted to the Pearl, Pascagoula, and Mobile bay drainages in Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, and Georgia (Page 1983)

 

Habitat: Fastest and deepest parts of main river channels with substrate made of uneven bedrock, pools, and drop-offs; many collection sites have rapids flowing over gravel, or rubble.

Climate/Range: Temperate; 35°N - 31°N

 

Biology:

            Feeding: Prey items include larval stages of aquatic insects and crustaceans (Rakocinski 1986); ninety percent of the diet volume comprises larger kinds of aquatic insects; Freckled Darter uses similar prey as the blackbanded and dusky darters, yet it consumes the upper end of the prey size spectrum.

            Reproduction:  Not much is known about freckled darter spawning, it may occur in early spring as females collected in May showed signs suggesting a spent condition; also it is believed that their eggs are fertilized externally.

 

Morphology:

            Body Shape: Large, spindle shaped, with a narrow caudal peduncle, and a wide premaxillary frenum; Mouth is horizontal, but not overhung by the snout.                                     Max. size: Largest species of darter; 194mm TL

           

            Life Colors: Back is dark brown or olive and is marbled with 9-10 interconnected darker blotches with light interspaces; Paired and medium fins have distinct black bands; there is a dark spot at the anterior base of both dorsal fins; undersides of the head and body are pale and dusky and are usually peppered with small melanophores, which are also prominent on the sides and pectoral base; however their color can quickly change from pale or blotched to very dark (Ross et al. 2001).

 

            External Morphology: Interorbital region is wide and flat, and the preopercular margin is serrated; the belly is fully scaled; the nape, opercle, and cheek have embedded or partially embedded scales; lateral line is straight and complete; with usually 3 or 4 pored scales extending onto the base of the caudal fin; Males have one to two rows of modified scales along the belly; 8 dark blotches on the back; 8-9 dark oval blotches on the sides, some of which may encircle the body posteriorly; paired and median fins have distinct black bands with a wide basal band; sometimes there is a narrow, clear basal band in the spinous dorsal fin. Some individuals have a dark streak along the belly; pre, post and suborbital bars are usually well developed (Ross et al. 2001)..

 

Morphologically similar species: The freckled darter is most similar to the dusky (P.sciera) and blackbanded (P.nigrofasciata) darters. It differs in its larger adult size, distinctive marbled pattern on the back and the dark spot at the anterior base of the second dorsal fin. Young freckled darters are separated from dusky and blackbanded darters by having the body encircled posteriorly by a pigment band passing through the anterior one-third of the dorsal and anal fins. The only other Percina that approach the freckled darter in size are the logperches, which can be distinguished by their overhanging, conical snouts and their narrow lateral bands (Ross et al. 2001).
