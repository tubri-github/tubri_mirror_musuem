Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

Moxostoma carinatum (Cope, 1870)

River Redhorse

 

                                                      © J. R. Tomelleri

                                                                                              

 

 

Conservation Status:

Louisiana Animal of Conservation Concern; It has a discontinuous distribution in the eastern half of the U.S. and a small part of southern Canada; uncommon to rare; has declined greatly from historic times; large river habitat makes protection difficult; vulnerable to major pollution events (such as toxic spills).

 

Federal T&E Status: NA

State Ranking: S1S3

NatureServe Global Ranking: G4

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution: Range from the Ohio River drainage into central tributaries of the Mississippi River Basin, north to Wisconsin and Minnesota, and south to Gulf Slope drainages from the Pearl River in Louisiana east to the Escambia River in Florida. Also found in the Great lakes and St. Lawrence River drainages (Ross et al. 2001). The distribution is discontinuous in the eastern half of the U.S. and a small part of southern Canada, probably due to loss of big river habitat between sites (Scott and Crossman 1973, Stagliano, D.M. 2001).

 

Habitat: Occur in large rivers, with moderate to strong currents and gravel or cobble substrates and are generally intolerant of silt. One major reason for the river redhorse’s intolerance of turbidity and siltation is that the major food items of this fish require clean gravel-sand stream bottoms and are very susceptible to reduction or extirpation of populations in response to excessive siltation (Burkhead and Jenkins 1991, Stagliano, D.M. 2001).Adult fish can occupy a wide range of habitats from shallow riffles to both shallow and deep areas of pools. Adult fish move into small streams during the spawning season (Ross et al. 2001).

Climate/Range: Temperate; 47°N - 31°N

 

Biology:

            Feeding: Major prey consists of snails and bivalve mollusks. Aquatic insects including mayflies, midges and caddisflies are also important food for adults and juveniles.

           

            Reproduction:  Spawning occurs in April and May at water temperatures of 15-24°C. Sexual maturity probably occurs at five years. Prior to spawning males move into runs where they maintain territory. Depressions in the gravel are created by substratum disturbance by the spawning fish. Two males pair with one female, settling to the bottom, with the female pressed in between the two males. The three fish “quiver” and the eggs are released, fertilized and buried in the gravel. (Ross et. al. 2001)

 

Morphology:

            Body Shape:  Large fusiform sucker; the caudal fin is forked; the dorsal fin is short, with a straight or slightly concave profile (Ross et al. 2001); The head is rounded between the eyes (convex) and the eyes are positioned near the midpoint of the head length. (ww.discoverlifeinamerica.org); one of the largest redhorses, with examples from the Flint River weighing 16lb (Tatum and Hackney 1970).

            Max. size: 737mmTL

           

            Life Colors: A brownish-grey dorsum, with sides becoming golden or brassy, and the undersides are white. Black pigment at the scale base on the back and middle portion of the body. Often the pigmentation is more intense on the scales on the back portion than middle portion. The caudal fin is a bright orange red, where the dorsal fin may be a paler red (Ross et al.).

 

            External Morphology: Plicate lips; subterminal mouth; posterior margin of the lower lip forms and angle which becomes wider in larger specimens (Ross et al. 2001). The scales are cycloid and of uniform size and the lateral line is complete and well developed (ww.discoverlifeinamerica.org).

 

Morphologically similar species: Moxostoma carinatum is most similar to the Golden (Moxostoma erythrurum) and Black (Moxostoma duquesnei), carinatum differs by having a thick pharyngeal arch with molarlike teeth, rather than a thin arch with comblike teeth. Also the River redhorse has dorsolateral scale pigmentation that is most dense at the base rather than at the margin or uniform. It can be distinguished from the Shorthead redhorse (Moxosotma macrolepidotum), which is the only other redhorse that develops bright red coloration on the caudal fin, by its straight or slightly concave dorsal fin margin, versus the falcate margin of the Shorthead. The River redhorse differs from the Blacktail redhorse (Moxostoma poecilurum), by lacking the distinct black and white caudal bands of the Blacktail. Finally Moxostoma carinatum sets itself apart from all other catostomids (suckers) by having a short dorsal fin (13-14 rays versus more than 20) and a complete and well developed lateral line. (Ross et al. 2001)
