Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

Polyodon spathula, (Walbaum, 1792)

 

                                                                                             

 

 

Conservation Status:

Louisiana Animal of Conservation Concern; P. spathula is apparently declining or of unknown trend in much of the range; threats include habitat alteration, pollution, and harvesting for caviar; being stocked in eleven states, largely to compensate for destruction or unavailability of spawning habitat.

 

Federal T&E Status: None

State Ranking: S3

NatureServe Global Ranking: G4

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution: North America: Mississippi River system, including the Missouri River into Montana, the Ohio River, and their major tributaries. At least one country reports adverse ecological impact after introduction. Currently found in 22 states that are part of the Mississippi River drainage (Mims, 2001). American paddlefish distribution is now restricted to this system of large, slow-moving rivers.

 

Habitat: Specifically adapted to large river systems and their associated floodplain lakes. In altered reaches of large rivers, fish occur in areas where they may find protection from strong currents, such as near dikes, revetments, or bridges (Southall and Hubert 1984; Russell 1986). Large river populations make extensive spawning migrations in the spring (Russell 1986; Paukert and Fisher 2000); their movement on these occasions associated with pools during high water, and with tailwater (where dams exist) and turbulent main-channel border habitats (Southall and Hubert 1984; Moen et al. 1992; Paukert and Fisher 2000).

Climate/Range/Temp: Temperate; 50°N - 30°N; 10°C - 18°C

 

Biology:  

            Feeding: During feeding paddlefish open their mouth very wide and actively swim through areas rich in plankton. The organisms are filtered from the water by the long, closely spaced gill rakers. (R.A. Rosen and Hales 1981) Feeding consists of two phases, ram suspension feeding and prey processing.

            Reproduction: Paddlefish are broadcast spawners whereby multiple males swim near and release milt onto the eggs liberated by a female. The eggs are very sticky and thus adhere to a substrate such as gravel or sand (Wills, 1993). Gravel and gravel plus cobble are the dominant substrates in spawning; velocity, depth, or substrate may be used as cover, either singly or in combination (Crance 1987; Wallus et al. 1990). Usually in swiftly flowing water over large gravel bars (Purkett 1961; 1963). Mature fish do not reproduce every year, but spawn every four to seven years (Vasetskiy 1971; Ross 2001). During spring spawning migrations, they may travel 160-322 km upriver, followed by downstream movements after spawning (Russell 1986; Pitman and Parks 1994; Paukert and Fisher 2001; Ross 2001; Stancill et al. 2002).          

 

Morphology:

            Body Shape:   Robust, with prominent paddle-like snout and strongly heterocercal tail; mouth large, not protrusible (Boschung and Mayden 2004); subterminal mouth; cartilaginous skeleton. Paddlefish from Lake Pontchartrain show some of the longest lengths at age for the species (B.C. Reed et al 1992). Max. size: 2.5 M   

           

            Life Colors: Dull colored and often mottled; color ranges from bluish-gray to black dorsally, and grades to lighter on the sides and white ventrally (Jennings and Zigler 2000)

           

            External Morphology: A distinctive rostrum accounts for approximately one-fourth to one-third of the length of the body; smaller fish have proportionally longer paddles (Ross 2001); maximum rostral development (as % TL) attained between 200-600 mm TL (Wallus et al. 1990). Smooth-bodied, generally lacking scales; very small scales may occur on the upper lobe of the heterocercal caudal fin, the base of the pectoral fin, or above the anterior portion of the lateral line (Russell 1986, Ross 2001). Two 3-4 mm long barbels present on underside of head in front of mouth (Ross 2001); gill cover extending posteriorly as long, pointed fleshy flap (Boschung and Mayden 2004). Male paddlefish can be distinguished from females by the papillae that surround the urogenital opening (Carlander 1969), which is somewhat raised in males, and is more flattened and softer in females (Meyer and Stevenson 1962). The paddle is covered with sense organs and likely functions as a tactile organ (Vasetskiy 1971), perhaps helping to locate high densities of planktonic organisms.
