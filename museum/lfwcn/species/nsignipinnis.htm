Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

 

Pteronotropis signipinnis, Bailey & Suttkus, 1952

Flagfin shiner

 

picture (Nosig_ui.jpg) by The Native Fish Conservancy

 

Synonymy:

Notropis signipinnis, (Bailey & Suttkus, 1952)

 

Conservation Status:

Are considered vulnerable in two states (Alabama and Louisiana)

out of the four the species occur in.

 

Federal T&E Status: Not evaluated

State Ranking: S3

NatureServe Global Ranking: G5

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution:  Mideastern North American drainages in Alabama, Florida, Louisiana, and Mississippi; primarily found in the Apalachiocola River and Pearl River watersheds.

 

Habitat: Freshwater; occur over sandy bottoms near aquatic vegetation in runs and pools of the headwaters of small streams and rivers.

 

Biology:

Feeding: Feeding activity rises after sunrise, peaking during midday, then declining rapidly after sunset. The food habits are unstudied, but observations would suggest they are omnivorous, feeding on both aquatic plants and invertebrates.

Reproduction: Spawning can extend from April to mid-August, with peak activity in June and July. During spawning, the male clasps the females by wrapping his caudal fin around her caudal peduncle. The eggs are broadcast over the substratum. (Ross, 2001)

 

Morphology:

Body Shape: The flagfin shiner is deep bodied and laterally compressed, with a rounded snout.

Maximum size: 7.0 cm TL (male/unsexed)

 

Life Colors:  P. signipinnis has yellow-orange fins and a broad blue-black lateral band running from the caudal fin base to the snout. The back is dark brown with a narrow, dark predorsal stripe, and the venter is white anterior to the anal fin. The dark lateral band is bordered above by an iridescent yellowish gold band. The bases of the anal and caudal fins are yellow, becoming orange-red, and the fin base varies from pale to intense yellow. (Ross, 2001)

 

External Morphology: The mouth of the flagfin shiner is terminal and oblique. The breast and belly for the fish are fully scaled. The lateral line s decurved and completely pored.

 

Morphologically similar species: The flagfin shiner is superficially very similar to the bluenose shiner which is often found where the flagfin is. The Flagfin is more than one-third of the body depth. Also the flagfin has a completely pored lateral line, a more compressed body and more anal rays. (Ross, 2001)
