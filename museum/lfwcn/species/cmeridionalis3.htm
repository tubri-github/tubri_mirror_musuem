Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

 

Cycleptus meridionalis, Burr & Mayden, 1999

Southeastern blue sucker

 

            Photo courtesy of The Encyclopedia of Alabama TM & © 2008

 

Synonymy:

Cyleptus elongatus, (non Lesueur, 1817)

 

Conservation Status:

Globally stable but habitat loss, especially in the Tombigee River system,

   is an increasing threat to the species.

 

Federal T&E Status: Not evaluated

State Ranking: S1

NatureServe Global Ranking: G3G4

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution:  North American Pearl, Pascagoula, Mississippi and Mobile basin drainages in Louisiana, Mississippi, and Alabama.

 

Habitat: Freshwater; occur in medium to large rivers systems with moderate to high flow.  Prefers deep channels and pools, but has been found in shallow vegetative areas near shore banks.                                                           

 

Biology:

Feeding: The diet of C. meridionalis is made up primarily on caddisfly, beetle, and midge larvae.

 

Reproduction: Spawn between February and April when water temperatures reach 12-14°C.

 

Morphology:

Body Shape:  The southeastern blue sucker is a large terete sucker with a moderately long snout.  

         Maximum size: 59.3 cm TL (male/unsexed); 70.2 cm TL (female)   

 

Life Colors: The back and upper sides are blue-black to dark gray, grading to white on the undersides.

 

External Morphology: C. meridionalis has small scales and a subterminal mouth with heavily papillose lips. The dorsal fin has one of the longest bases of the catostomids, with an extremely falcate profile. (Ross, 2001)

 

Morphologically similar species:

The coloration of Cycleptus meridionalis is essentially the same as that of Cycleptus elongatus. They can be separated because the southeastern blue sucker has fewer predorsal circumferential scales and fewer circumferential scales on the caudal peduncle. It differs from all other suckers with a high lateral scale count and long dorsal fin.
