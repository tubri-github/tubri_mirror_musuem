Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

 

Percina aurora, Suttkus & Thompson, 1994

Pearl darter

 

http://www.conservationfisheries.org/species_accounts/Percina_aurora.htm

Photo by Conservation Fisheries © 2007

 

Synonymy:

None

 

Conservation Status:

Habitat loss from siltation, dredging, and pollution have contributed to this species being critically imperiled. 

More information is needed for this species.

 

Federal T&E Status: Not evaluated

State Ranking: SH

NatureServe Global Ranking: G1

 

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution:  North America; Louisiana and Mississippi; known from Pearl and Pascagoula River drainages.  Possibly extirpated from the Pearl River drainage.

 

Habitat: Freshwater; inhabits pools and riffles in medium to large streams and rivers. Substrate usually consists of gravel or cobble and current is swift.

 

Biology:

Feeding:Benthic; feed on chironomids and small crustaceans.

 

Reproduction: Spawning occurs from late February or March to May. Males activly defend a territory, which includes spawning, feeding, and refuge sites. After spawning, the fish leave the margins of the streamsand move into deeper water.

 

Morphology:

Body Shape: A small darter with a blunt sout, horizontal mouth,  and large eyes situated high on the head.

 

Life Colors:  Percina aurora has a medial black caudal spot at the base of the caudal fin. Also there are 6-9 irregular dark blotches along the sides. The upper sides have dark x-shaped markings. The spinous dorsal fin has a dark basal band, where as the second dorsal fin is lightly spotted. As well there are two to four dark vertcal bars on the caudal fin.

 

External Morphology: A premaxillary frenum is geneally absent. The opercle of the pearl darter is scaled, the nape is unscaled anteriorly,the breast is unscaled except for a single median scale, and the cheek is scaled to unscaled. Males have an elavated spinous and soft dorsal  fins, and enlarged causal fin and a longer upper jaw than females. (Ross, 2001)

 

Morphologically similar species:

 The pearl darter could be confused with the blackside darter, which also has a distinct caudal spot. It differs from the blackside darter in having and unscaled nape anterioirly, in usually lacking a frenum, and in having 11 or fewer dorsal spines. The species could also be confused with the superficially similar speckled or johnny darters, which lack the modified belly scales charecteristic of he genus Percina. (Ross, 2001)
