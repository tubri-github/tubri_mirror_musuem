Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

Cyprinella camura (Jordan and Meek, 1884)

Blunt-face Shiner

 

 

Synonymy: Notropis camurus (Jordan and Meek, 1884)                                                                                           

 

 

Conservation Status: Louisiana Animal of Conservation Concern

 

Federal T&E Status: NA

State Ranking: S2S3

NatureServe Global Ranking: G5

 

Habitat and Biology

 

Distribution: Occurs in two disjunct populations, one east of the Mississippi River in west flowing tributaries, primarily in Tennessee, Mississippi, and Louisiana. The other population is west of the Mississippi River in Oklahoma, Kansas, Arkansas, and Missouri (Ross et al. 2001).

 

Habitat: Common in clear streams with moderate to swift flow and sand or gravel substrate (Ross et al. 2001). Eastern populations may occur over sand; in west, typically over gravel or rubble (Mayden 1989, Page and Burr 1991). Generally not found in low gradient habitats. Adults are generally in fast riffles and raceways; young typically in or close to these habitats but also in shallow pools (Mayden 1989).

Climate/Range: Subtropical; 39°N - 30°N

 

Biology:

            Feeding:  The bluntface shiner breads on small invertebrates similar to other know Cyprinella.

           

            Reproduction: Spawning occurs from late March through mid-August (Ross et al. 2001). Bluntface shiners are crevice spawners as is true of other Cyprinella species( Mayden 1989)

 

Morphology:

            Body Shape: Deep bodied, laterally compressed, with a small triangular shaped head; the dorsal and ventral profiles of the body are about equally curved, and the greatest body depth is at the dorsal fin origin. Snout length is proportionally greater in larger fish, so that the eye diameter is smaller than the snout length once fish have reached about 65-70 mm SL. (Ross et al. 2001)

            Max. size: 107 mm SL; 150 mm TL

           

            Life Colors: The back and upper sides are olive-brown becoming silvery on the flanks and sides of the head. The belly is silvery white. The interradial membranes of the dorsal fin have black pigmentation that becomes more intense towards the posterior of the fin. The posterior 2/3’s of the caudal fin is dusky with the basal third being milky white in color. Pigmentation on the dorsal and lateral scales results in a strong cross-hatching pattern. Breeding males are bright steel blue along the back and upper sides and have well developed blue-black scapular bars; white tips on the caudal fin and on the edge of the anterior dorsal fin rays; reddish snouts; and some red on the leading edge of the dorsal fin (Ross et al. 2001).

 

            External Morphology: C. camura has an oblique, terminal to slightly subterminal mouth; the dorsal fin origin is above or just posterior to the pelvic fin origin. The breast and belly are completely scaled; Breeding males develop enlarged dorsal fins that are rounded posteriorly (Ross et al. 2001).

 

Morphologically similar species: The bluntface shiner is found with three similar looking species of Cyprinella, the blacktail (Cyprinella venusta), the red (Cyprinella lutrensis), and the steelcolor (Cyprinella whipplei) shiners. Camura can be distinguished from the blacktail because it lacks the distinctive black caudal spot, and usually has nine (versus 8) anal rays. It can be distinguished from the red shiner because the bluntface shiner has more lateral scales and is less deep bodied than the red shiner. The only reliable characteristic for differentiating between the bluntface and steelcolor shiners is the caudal pigmentation and the pattern of breeding tubercles (steelcolors have a distinct hiatus between the snout and the interorbital tubercles versus no separation in the bluntface shiner) (Ross et al. 2001).
