Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

 

Notropis boops, Gilbert, 1884

Bigeye shiner

 

picture by Darren Thornbrugh, Kansas, USA 2007

 

Synonymy:

None

 

Conservation Status:

Globally the species is considered abundant, however, in West Virginia and Mississippi local state agencies

have declared the species critically imperiled. Four other states

(Alabama, Ohio, Illinois, and Kansas) have also elevated the species status to imperiled.

 

Federal T&E Status: Not evaluated

State Ranking: S3

NatureServe Global Ranking: G5

 

Habitat and Biology

Distribution: North America in the Lake Erie drainage and Middle Mississippi River basin.

 

Habitat: Freshwater; found in clear flowing streams and rivers with rocky pools and ripples.  Are also usually present in shallow vegetated areas along stream margins.

 

Biology:

Feeding: N. boops forms schools and actively feeds on the mid to upper water column. Major prey includes terrestrial and aquatic insects, organic detritus and algae. The contribution of algae to the diet increases in the summer and fall.

Reproduction: Spawning occurs from April through August, although actual breeding times may vary with yearly changes to water temperature. Most fish only spawn during a single spawning season. (Ross, 2001)

 

Morphology:

Body Shape:  Notropis boop is a medium sized minnow with a distinctly large eye. It also has a short bluntly rounded snout. The dorsal fin is positioned over the pelvic insertion equidistant between the snout and caudal fin base. The dorsal fin is also slightly pointed and when the fin is depressed the anterior rays are longer than the posterior rays. The belly and breast of N. boops are fully scaled, although the scales on the breast may be embedded. (Ross, 2001)

Maximum size: 9.0 cm TL (male/unsexed) 

 

Life Colors:  The back and upper sides are greenish. The dorsal scales are distinctly outlined with dark brown-back pigment and their centers lack pigment. The thin mid-dorsal dorsal stripe is somewhat expanded at the dorsal fin origin. A dark lateral band encircles the snout and continues posteriorly to the caudal fin base, where it may produce a weak, elongate caudal spot. (Ross, 2001)

 

 

External Morphology: The bigeye shiner has a rather large, terminal, oblique mouth. Its lateral line is decurved anteriorly and punctulate.

 

Morphologically similar species:  

Due to its lateral band, the bigeye shiner may appear similar to a number of cyprinids, including the weed and mimic shiners and the pugnose minnow. The bigeye shiner can be distinguished by a combination of its distinctly large eye, black peritoneum, eight anal rays, and a 1,4-4,1 pharyngeal tooth count. (Ross, 2001)
