Louisiana Fish Species of Concern

Status Report

 

Cycleptus elongates (Lesueur, 1817)

Blue Sucker

            

                                                                                          

 

 

Conservation Status:

Louisiana Animal of Conservation Concern; Vulnerable because it has a wide distribution in large rivers in central North America; locally common in some areas, but greatly reduced in abundance in other parts of the range as a result of dam construction and reductions in water quality.

 

Federal T&E Status: NA

State Ranking: S2S3

NatureServe Global Ranking: G3G4

 

Habitat and Biology

 

Distribution: Found in large rivers from the Rio Grande drainage to the eastern tributaries of the Mississippi River. (Ross et al. 2001)

 

Habitat: Deep channels of moderate to large, free-flowing rivers; can be found in the main channel and along the revetted and naturally steep banks of the lower Mississippi river (J.A. Baker et al. 1991). Found over cobble and/or bedrock substrates; adults occupy deep riffles (typically 1-2 m depth) in areas of very swift flow, with current speeds from 100-260 cm/s; juveniles occupy shallower, less swift water (Moss et al. 1983)

Climate/Range: Subtropical; 47°N - 28°N

 

Biology:

            Feeding:  The blue sucker’s diet consists primarily of larval and pupal stages of aquatic insects, especially those of the caddisflies and midges. (Ross et al. 2001) There is also evidence of mayfly larvae and amphipods being eaten (Rupprecht and Jahn 1980), as well as hellgrammites and fingernail clams (R.E. Moss et al 1983). The items consumed indicates that most feeding takes place over a firm substratum (Cowley and Sublette 1987) young fish feed on smaller insect larvae, such as midges, black flies, and caddisflies (R.E. Moss et al. 1983). 

           

            Reproduction:  Spawning is believed to occur in the late spring and early summer, based on male tuberculation, the presence of larvae and gonadal condition (Walburg et al. 1971; Rupprecht and Jahn 1980; Muth and Schmulbach 1984; Ross et al. 2001). The downstream migration of larvae suggests an upstream spawning migration by adults. Spawning occurs in deep riffles (1-2 m) with cobble and bedrock substrates; water velocities at spawning sites were 1.8 m/sec. and males move into spawning areas before the females (Moss et al. 1983).

 

Morphology:

            Body Shape:  Terete; extremely muscular and heavy-bodied (Moss et al. 1983). Elongate body, oval in cross section (Burr and Mayden 1999). Eye closer to back of head than to tip of snout; head abruptly more slender than body, in adults; dorsal fin base more than one-third of standard length (Hubbs et al. 2008)

            Max. size:  825 mm SL (Burr and Mayden 1999); 927 mm TL (Carlander 1969)

           

            Life Colors: Life colors of male olive blue or slate olive on dorsum and sides of body with brassy reflections; No significant color differences between males and females (Burr and Mayden 1999); the caudal fin is bicolored, with the upper lobe unpigmented and the lower lobe densely pigmented; anal fin coloration is highly variable, with pigmentation becoming darker with increasing size. Nuptial males are intensely blue-black. (Ross et al. 2001)

 

            External Morphology: A large sucker with a long snout, small scales and a subterminal mouth with heavily papillose lips. The lateral line is complete and curved upward anteriorly. The dorsal fin has one of the largest bases among catostomids, with a falcate profile; the anal fin has a nearly vertical profile when extended; pectoral and pelvic fins are large and falcate, with the first two rays of both fins thickened (Ross et al. 2001). Caudal fin in adults is large, widely forked, the lobe tips are slightly rounded to pointed, and the inner margins are straight; both lobes are about equal in length (Burr and Mayden 1999).

 

Morphologically similar species: The genus Cycleptus is not likely to be confused with other catostomids, differing from all other suckers in the high lateral line scale count (greater than 49) and long dorsal fin. The blue sucker is not sympatric with the southeastern blue sucker (Cycleptus meridionalis). (Ross et al. 2001)
