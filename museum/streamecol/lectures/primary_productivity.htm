PRIMARY PRODUCTION AND COMMUNITY RESPIRATION

Primary production is the rate of production of organic matter (fixed carbon) from inorganic carbon by photosynthesizing organisms (units = mass/(area [or volume] x time)). Thus, it represents the transformation of solar energy to reduced chemical energy. The total amount of energy produced is gross primary productivity (GPP). Of course the plant uses some of the energy in its own respiration (Ra). The balance is stored as plant biomass and is called net primary productivity or NPP.

Thus,

GPP = NPP + Ra

In streams, the source of carbon for plant biomass production is CO2 dissolved in water. Oxygen is produced in the process. Thus, it is possible to measure the rate of primary production by measuring changes in CO2 and O2 (usually) concentrations in water. Another method is to measure the rate of uptake of radioactively labeled [14C] bicarbonate. The 14C uptake method provides productivity measures between GPP and NPP. It is more sensitive than the gas exchange method and thus provides better estimates when primary productivity is low.

A number of stream ecologists use an in situ method of determining gas (O2) exchange rate based on the equation:

Q(dissolved O2) = P - R +/- E,

where P = change in [O2] due to photosynthesis, R = change due to respiration, and E = change due to exchange with the atmosphere (assumes negligible accrual from surface and groundwater inputs). The change due to respiration includes both autotroph and heterotroph (Rh) respiration. The two of these respiration rates combined is called community respiration (CR). CR can be measured only at night (measurement confounded in daytime by plant O2 production). Thus, daily CR (CR24) is estimated by extrapolating average nighttime CR through the daylight hours.

Estimates of GPP derived in this way will be subject to error from (1) differences in light and dark respiration, including the inability to account for photorespiration by plants (light-dependent O2 uptake and CO2 evolution, and excretion of photosynthate); (2) inability to measure autotrophic respiration separately; and (3) any asynchrony between photosynthesis and associated metabolic costs.

Net daily metabolism (NDM) can be estimated from the difference between GPP and CR24:

NDM = GPP - CR24

NDM is free of errors associated with estimation of daytime respiration. If GPP exceeds CR24, then there is net energy gain to the system; if CR24 exceeds GPP then there is net energy loss. It is for this reason the NDM is also referred to as net ecosystem productivity.

In small to medium-sized streams, the greatest portion of biomass, and therefore the greatest metabolism, is in the benthose. In large rivers, water column biomass (plankton) dominates productivity. In some systems, macrophyte (large vascular plant) productivity can be significant.

Respirometer chambers, equipped for water recirculation, may be used to measure metabolism (introduced for use with benthose). Although free of the need to correct for gas exchange, there are concerns about how chamber design influences nutrients, water flow, and the ratio of sample surface to water volume, relative to the in situ method.

14C uptake method has been little used in streams. This is because corrections must be made for adsorption of isotope onto surfaces and for proportions of photosynthate excreted by the producer as labeled organic matter. The method is best applied in closed systems.
