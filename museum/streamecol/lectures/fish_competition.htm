HABITAT USE AND COMPETITION IN STREAM FISHES

Community ecologists are interested in understanding how is it that so many kinds of organisms can coexist in space and food-limited environments. They theorize that organisms specialize in morphology and life habits so that they don't have the same needs (occupy different niches), or they occupy habitats at different times or live in different habitats so they won't overlap in resource use.

It is true that fishes and other stream organisms exhibit a variety of body forms, life habits, and habitats. Some fishes are specialized to live in fast water, others are better suited to live in slack water. Some live on the stream bottom, others live in the water column. Some favor the shore areas along the sides of streams, others prefer mid channel. Some are active by day, some by night.

An important question in stream fish community ecology (as for any area of community ecology) is: How did these differences in body form and life habits come about? Are they the result of interspecific competition ("ghosts of competition past"), or are they independently evolved traits that in effect preadapt species for coexistence?

When you were collecting fishes from our study streams, did you notice differences in habitats where minnows were coming from? When you sorted and identified the minnows, did you notice any morphological differences among species that would serve to segregate the species in terms of types of food eaten or area of the water column inhabited?

Over long periods of time, we can imagine that closely related species that evolved in different places (i.e., diverged from a common ancestor) can come to occupy the same stream, and even share the same habitat. In fact, it is common for species that share similar phylogeny, morphology, behavior, and ecological requirements to occur together in the same habitat, and thus have the potential to compete.

One of the ways species avoid competition is through habitat partitioning: subdivision of habitat space based on microhabitat features. Thus, within a riffle, one species might be specialized to live in the fastest portion of the riffle, while another might favor slower portions. You might expect, therefore, that these microhabitats would differ in water velocity, substrate composition (larger substrates in fast water), depth, and accumulations of detrital material. At a broader scale one species may favor riffles in small streams, whereas another may favor riffles in large streams.

Stream fishes clearly segregate in these ways. The question is: Are they segregating in this way because of ongoing or past in situ competition, or are they expressing historically evolved traits? One of the best ways to investigate this is through natural experiments: quantify the habitat use of a species in the presence or absence of potential competitors; then look for evidence of a shift in habitat association in the presence of a competitor.

Gorman (1988) did an experimental study of habitat use in an assemblage of Ozark minnows. A previous field study had shown that species were segregated in pools primarily on the basis of a single habitat variable: position in the water column. Lack of control over species combinations and densities prevented Gorman from determining the relative contribution of habitat preference versus interspecific competition to habitat use patterns. Aquaria were used to simulate pool habitats in a series of laboratory experiments designed to determine the prefered vertical distribution of six minnow species. Three of the species had distinct vertical position preferences. A series of pairwise and multispecies experiments were then conducted to determine the effect of interspecific association on vertical position preference. Thirty four of 45 possible pairwise interactions were significant (18 positive associations; 16 negative associations). Mean pairwise overlaps were larger for positive associations than for negative assocations. Interactions were complex in three and four-species experiments. Species exhibited flexibility in their vertical distributions, adjusting their position in response to certain heterospecifics (as identified in pairwise associations). The flexibility in habitat use observed in the laboratory explained the variability oberved in the field and suggested that interspecies interactions (positive and negative) were important determinants of habitat use patterns.
