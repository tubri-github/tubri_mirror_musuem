THE RIVER CONTINUUM CONCEPT (RCC)

·Is an attempt to explain the zonation that is seen in stream systems

·first presented by Vannote, et al. (1980), the original idea was that of Vannote

·The main concepts include:

            Streams are a continua of mosaics

-of intergrading population aggregates

-of physical and chemical gradients

it describes the structure and function of communities along the river system

-energy inputs

-organic matter transport

-energy storage

-use of energy by the streams functional feeding groups

-fluvial geomorphic processes

•streams have been described as open systems in dynamic equilibrium

•producers and consumers in a given reach conform to the geomorphology of the river itself

oThe RCC proposes broad characteristics of lotic communities according to headwaters, medium rivers, large rivers

-Headwater streams-

•                       shaded by riparian vegetation

*reduces autotrophic production

•riparian vegetation contributes large quantities of allochthonous material

•depend on terrestrial energy inputs

•so P/R < 1 (i.e., heterotrophic community)

•community primarily based on the following functional feeding groups:

*shredders-biters and chewers; take large food and produce small foods. Herbivorous or detritivorous (leaves and microfauna)

*collector-gatherers- spin nets or use setea to collect organic matter. Filter w/ nets, hairs, cephalic fans (balck flies)

-Medium Streams -

•rely on rooted vegetation or algae

•P/R > 1 (i.e., autotrophic community)

*zone of shifting from heterotrophic to autotrophic depends on

+opening of canopy,

+increased width of stream

+usually third order stream

•community largely consists of

*collector-gatherers- see above

*scrapers (grazers) - feed on aufwuchs (substrate) specialized mouth parts to scrape materials or substrate.

-

-Large Rivers-

•get large quantities of Fine Particulate Organic Material (FPOM) from upstream

*through "leakage"

•effect of riparian vegetation is insignificant due to width of stream

•limited by depth and turbidity

•P/R < 1 (shift back to heterotrophic community)

•smaller rivers entering large rivers have localized effects

•community largely collector-gatherer (i.e., filter feeder) based

•also begin to see true river plankton (potamoplankton); both zoo- and phytoplankton

Predatory species change little in relative abundance along stream orders

Fish shift from cool-cold water species to warm water species

There are associated changes in organic compounds and in indices of diversity

-relative abundance of soluble organic compounds is

•high in headwaters due to transport from terrestrial system

•are quickly absorbed by organisms inheadwaters

•stays low through from third order size on

-species diversity indices

•low in headwater streams because

*of narrow temperature regime (stenothermal)

*restricted nutritional base

•increases by third order due to

*increase of temperature variations (eurythermal)

*increase of variation of nutritional base (i.e., different types of food)

•begins to decline in large river because

*uniformity of habitat

*uniformity of food types

*again greater uniformity of temperature regime due to large volume of water

·In conclusion

oRCC suggests changes in community structure and function

ochanges are a function of changes in geomorphic, physical and biotic variables such as

-stream flow

-channel morphology (especially width and depth)

-detritus loading

-size of available POM

-thermal regimes

-changes in autotrophic production

-changes in nutritional sources
