NUTRIENT ENRICHMENT

Most ecosystems respond to nutrient addition with increased production, suggesting that they are nutrient limited. In standing-water (lentic) habitats, excessive nutrient loading causes the system to become eutrophic: biological production becomes so excessive that it deprives water of needed oxygen.

Streams typically are not thought of as high-oxygen, nutrient-limited systems. Dissolved nutrient concentrations are typically very low in pristine streams. However, the levels are probably sufficient for stream autotrophs because the flowing water means constant replenishment of nutrients. What happens when we add nutrients to streams in excess of ambient levels?

The focus of this discussion will be on approaches to studying effects of nutrient enrichment on periphyton production, because this is the component of the stream biota that is most sensitive to nutrient dynamics.

A number of different approaches are used to assess nutrient regulation of periphyton production in streams. We will consider four approaches: 1) in situ nutrient manipulation; 2) enrichment of flow through enclosures; 3) whole-stream manipulations; and 4) integrated bioassays. The different approaches represent different spatial scales, and different degrees of controlled experimentation versus natural system response.

In situ nutrient manipulation involves deploying nutrient-rich substrata and measuring periphyton growth response in stream (nutrient rich substrata design figure). Substrata are typically dishes filled with sterile sand and/or agar enriched with nitrate, phosphate, both, or nothing (control). Ideally, the substrata should simulate nutrient-diffusing action of certain natural stream substrates (e.g., chironomid-dwelling tubes in sand).   After a prescribed length of time, the dishes are collected and periphyton growth is measured by oxidizing the substrates and measuring AFDM.

Another approach involves use of flow-through enclosures (flumes). The enclosures are pipes filled with natural or artificial substrates with open or closed bottoms that are placed either in the streambed parallel to the flow, or beside the stream with pumped stream flow (flow through design figure). Nutrients are dripped in as water flows through. After the experiment has run its course, the substrates are collected and periphyton growth in nutrient treatments is compared to that in controls.

Whole-stream manipulations involve adding nutrients to the whole stream and measuring the response of periphyton growth on ceramic tiles placed upstream and downstream of the nutrient entry point. This approach has the advantage of being the most natural, but the disadvantage of being the most difficult to control.

The integrated approach simply involves combining different approaches. This allows for comparison of nutrient effects across different scales.

As mentioned, streams are naturally nutrient-limited and tend to be oxygen saturated.  Thus, they have a natural capacity to endure a fair amount of nutrient loading.  However, anthropogenic nutrient addition - especially around urban and agricultural areas - can be so excessive that it quickly exhausts the natural buffering capacity of streams, resulting in eutrophication.  Phosphorus addition tends to be highest around urban areas and row-crop agricultural areas.  Nitrogen addition is highest around animal production and sewerage treatment plants.  Excess nutrients cause biomass and biological oxygen demand to increase.  DO decreases to point where it favors certain “tolerant” macrofauna (inverts such as black flies and chironomids, fish such as bluegill and mosquitofish). Algal communities become dominated by unpalatable blue-green algae.  Harmful bacteria increase to levels where they are harmful to biota.  Eventually, the stream reach receiving the nutrients effectively dies.  Conditions improve with distance from pollution source.
