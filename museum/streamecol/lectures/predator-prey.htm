PREDATOR-PREY INTERACTIONS

Predation-prey interactions are typically viewed as secondary consumption (animals eating other animals). Predator-prey theory deals with the impact of predators on prey populations and prey community composition. The central question is: Can predators influence the structure of prey populations/communities? Most predator-prey theory was developed for closed, equilibrium systems. Streams are a patchwork of open, nonequilibrium habitats (riffles, pools) connected by migration. At the habitat patch scale, the question is: How does predation in the patch influence prey population/ community composition within the patch? At this scale, predators are not thought of as capable of structuring prey populations/communities. Models developed for these systems (e.g., Caswell 1978) predict coexistence of predator and prey populations for long periods of time, but suggest that predators can have a major structuring influence.

The effects of predators on prey populations depend on the predation rate and its relationship to prey exchange rate (rate at which prey move in and out of areas where predators are feeding. In stream reaches where background rates of prey migration are high (high drift rates), the effects of predation may be swamped by prey immigration. Where background migration rates are low, or where predators induce high rates of prey emigration, the impacts of predators are more easily substantiated. Similarly, the impact of predators on different prey species may vary depending on relative prey vulnerability, background migration, and tendency of prey to emigrate from patches where predators are foraging.

The effects of predators and prey can extend well beyond direct, predator-induced mortality. Predators can have indirect, community-level effects such as top-down effects or trophic cascades (predators effects community composition below the level of their prey), and nonlethal effects that affect prey fitness, such as where predator avoidance by prey causes non-ideal feeding, or shifts to unfavorable habitats.

The mechanisms or specific causes of predator effects on prey communities often depend on whether predators are selective (i.e., consumption of a certain type of prey is out of proportion to the prey's density in the environment). Understanding these mechanisms is the purview of behavioral and evolutionary ecology. Selective predation may result from concentration of predator search in a preferred prey habitat, active rejection of certain types of prey, or differential prey vulnerability. Selection that occurs because of predator rejection is a function of predator choice (active behavioral selection); selection that occurs because of differential vulnerability is more a function of stereotypical predator behavior (fixed behavioral selection), prey defenses (passive selection), or microhabitat differences among prey that affect vulnerability.

A study by Sih and Wooster (1994) examined the relationship of prey behavior and prey dispersal on predator impacts in streams. Previous model suggested that prey exchange (movement of prey among habitat patches) reduces predator impact on prey density. However, the model included two assumptions that influenced its qualitative predictions: 1) it assumed that the surrounding environment had no predators and constant prey density; 2) it assumed that prey do not alter their per capita emigration rates in response to the presence of predators. Sih and Wooster sought to refine the model by considering the impacts of these assumptions (relaxing them and examining the effects).

They found that prey emigration behavior had a profound effect on the relationship between prey exchange and predator impact. If prey do not alter their emigration rates in response to predators, then high prey exchange rates result in low predator impact (as predicted by the previous model). However, if prey disperse out of patches in response to predators, then even very high prey exchange rates cannot swamp predator impact (in fact, prey emigration adds to predator impact). If predators force prey to hide (thereby suppressing prey emigration), then high prey exchange can result in "negative predator impacts" (higher prey density in patches with predators).

Invertebrate predators (stoneflies) tended to have stronger effects on prey (always reducing prey density) than vertebrate predators (effects mixed). Studies suggest that invertebrate predators decrease prey density mostly by increasing invertebrate drift rates; whereas, vertebrates (fish) tend to decrease prey activity (thereby limiting prey response). Drift makes prey less vulnerable to invertebrate predators (benthic, tactile feeders), but more vulnerable to fish (sight feeders). This causes what Sih and Wooster called a "paradox of danger", wherein more dangerous predators (fish) have weaker impacts on prey populations than less dangerous predators (invertebrates).

Predator-prey interactions often lead to evolution of a complex array of prey defenses. A study by Crowl and Covich (1990) showed that the widely distributed pulmonate snail Physella virgata changed its life history in response to the presence of a crayfish predator, Orconectes virilis. The changes were apparently initiated by a water-borne cue released when crayfish fed on the snails. In the presence of the cue, snails exhibited rapid growth and delayed reproduction 8 months until they reached a size of about 10 mm. In the absence of the cue, snails reproduced after only 3.5 months at a size of 4 mm. The chemically induced shift indicates that life history of the snail is phenotypically plastic. Snails were apparently able to reduce predation pressure by increasing age and size at maturity.
