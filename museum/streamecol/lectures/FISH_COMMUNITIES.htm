FISH COMMUNITY COMPOSITION

Fishes are perhaps the most obvious component of the stream biota. They are certainly the dominant organisms in terms of size, and seem to dominate biomass in many streams as well.

Just as with invertebrates, ecologists think of stream fishes as organized in communities - a group of species inhabiting a prescribed area and interacting with each other and other groups of organisms in some important way. It is common practice (though completely unnatural) to study stream fish communities as separate from other components of stream biota (stream fish community ecology).

As with invertebrates, stream fish communities have emergent properties of richness (the number of species present in the community), diversity (a broader concept that includes numbers and relative abundances of species), and complexity of trophic and other functional roles. Studies of stream fish communities have two basic steps: description of the community (community composition) and community processes and interactions. Most of this discussion deals with the first of these steps.

The composition of any community is determined a combination of historical and ecological processes. Historical processes, such as speciation and dispersal of species from one region to another, represent first-order determinants of community composition because they determine the size of the pool of available species a particular local community is drawn from. These events are influenced by geological (orogeny), climatic (glaciation, sea level rise or fall) and hydrological (drainage changes) activities.

 

Ecological processes, such as physiological tolerances of species, environmental disturbance, interspecific competition and other forms of biological interaction, represent second-order determinants of community composition, because they influence (limit) the number of species a particular area can support. An important assumption in this line of reasoning is that there is a limit on the number of species an area can support, based on space and available resources, and the adaptations of species.

 

It has long been demonstrated that stream fish diversity increases from headwaters to mouth in streams, and that species are generally added to communties rather than replaced (longitudinal zonation). Small streams are generally considered harsh, unpredictable environments for stream fishes because they are temporally variable and prone to extremes of flow (floods and droughts). Disturbance is thus thought to be an important factor limiting fish diversity in small streams. Large streams are more stable and predictable environments than small streams, and are thus expected to support more species. Many studies have shown that stream fish diversity increases as a function of habitat complexity and habitat depth or volume. Large streams generally offer a larger variety of habitats and more habitat volume than small streams, and are thought to support more species for this reason. The notion here is that the greater the variety of habitats an area has, the more species it can support.

 

The above relationships are well established and useful for understanding diversity variation within stream systems. However, disturbance and habitat complexity variation alone are not sufficient to explain fish diversity differences among stream systems.

 

Warm-water streams of the eastern United States have a particular taxonomic composition. They tend to be dominated by minnows (family Cyprinidae) and have high representation of sunfishes (family Centrarchidae), darters (family Percidae), catfishes (family Ictaluridae), and suckers (family Catostomidae). Minnows, suckers, and catfishes are members of a group of fishes (ostariophysans) that dominate freshwater communities around the world. Darters and sunfishes are more recently evolved descendants of acanthomorph marine fishes, but rival ostariophysans in diversity in the eastern United States.

 

Composition of stream fish communities can be characterized in terms of functional and trophic roles. Some stream fishes are bottom-oriented (benthic), some pelagic (nektonic), others surface-oriented (neustonic). Some have strictly herbivorous diets, feeding on stream algae (e.g., stonerollers). Some feed on insect larvae (insectivores), straining them from bottom sediments or picking them from the water column (drift). Some feed on benthic macroinvertebrates (insects, crustaceans, clams, snails); others feed primarily on other fishes (piscivores). The relative proportions of different functional and trophic groups vary based on environmental conditions.
