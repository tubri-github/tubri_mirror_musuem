LAND USE AND STREAMS

Mention was made early on that streams are formed from catchments that include all of the land area surrounding the stream and its tributaries. The stream and the land that feeds it are collectively referred to as a watershed, and there are important linkages between these two components that influence hydrology, temperature, energy and nutrient inputs to streams. This discussion is about the impact of anthropogenic modification of land surrounding a stream on the stream ecosystem.

Mentioned more recently that channelization of streams was the most pervasive form of human modication of streams. Impacts from land use changes are a very close second. Land cover in watersheds is altered to differing degrees and intensities by humans, with differing degrees of impacts on streams. Urbanization is the most intensive and has the greatest impact. Certain types of forestry (e.g., selective cutting) are the least intensive and impact streams the least. Agricultural land use (many forms and intensities of land use within this) is intermediate between these other types of land use, but is so widespread that it receives most of the attention.

Modification of watersheds for agriculture involves removal of natural vegetation and reshaping of the stream itself to improve drainage. In the eastern United States, removal of natural vegetation usually involved deforestation, including forests of the riparian zone, which are the most important to streams. In agricultural states such as Illinois, it is no almost impossible to image what the land and streams were like in their natural state. Crops extend to the edges of very straight stream channels. There are almost no trees.

Agricultural runoff is the main source of pollution of streams and other aquatic habitats. The largest single polluntant is soil eroded from the land. 80% of the 4.9 billion metric tons of soil eroded from rural, non-federal land came from cropland. The second largest pollutant is nutrients. Agricultural sources are responsible for half of the phosphorous and nitrogen discharged to U.S. waterways. Because these polluntants originate over large land areas, they are not traceable to a particular source (hence the name non-point source pollution).

Agricultural land use in watersheds generally causes streams to become shallower and more uniform in depth and substrate composition due to sedimentation. Streams become warmer due to removal of canopy vegetation and depth profile changes. They become more dependent on autochthonous (primary) production (decreased allochthonous inputs of CPOM from trees), and hydrologically more unpredicable (fed more by runoff than hyporheic flow).

In larger rivers, floodplains are altered by clearing of trees, draining and filling in of back swamps, leveeing of the river to prevent overbank flooding. Floodplains and lateral aquatic habitats are critical to the ecology of large rivers. We have yet to fully assess how reduction of these habitats has impacted large river ecosystems. It is clear that the nutrients originating from agricultural and urban sources are not being consumed while in transport, because excessive nutrient loading is causing eutrophication and other problems in coastal estuaries at the mouth of the Mississippi and other large rivers.

Animal production is another form of agricultural land use that impacts streams. In this case, natural forest or native grassland vegetation is typically converted to common feed grasses, which are typically heavily fertilized. Animals grazing on the land causes soil compaction. In cases where land is overgrazed, grasses usually fail and soil erodes. Animal wastes are a source of nutrients to streams draining animal lands. Stream receiving runoff from chicken houses, hog farms, cattle feed lots, or slaughter yards are heavily impacted.

Silviculture (timber production) is generally viewed as a less intensive form of land use, however forestry practices vary widely and can have dramatic impacts of streams. The forest practices with the greatest impacts on streams are clear cutting and road building. It is important to keep in mind that forestry today is usually practiced in areas where the land is steeply sloped (low land forests were cut long ago). Clear cutting clears forest from large areas of this land. The tree cover not only protects the land from erosion, but also helps water to penetrate the soil, plays a key role in the water cycle (evapotranspiration), absorbs and reflects solar radiation (influencing ground temperature) and contributes litter to the forest floor. Without this cover, the land is easily eroded and subject to rapid water runoff, exposed to high solar heating, and robbed of nutrients. Streams draining clear-cut land are subject to flash flooding and complete drying in low water periods, heavy sedimentation, high heating, and reduced food energy.

Forest road building on steep hillslopes often leads to hillside failure. The roads often cut unnaturally across stream drainage systems, and act themselves as conduits of runoff.  Stream crossings are culverts or pipes which disrupt fish movements.  

The general biological impact of agricultural and silvicultural land use is reduced abundance and diversity of stream organisms due to reduced and degraded instream habitat.
