STREAM MODIFICATION: CHANNELIZATION

Humans have been modifying streams to suit their needs since the beginning of human existence. Stream modification is a general term for any physical modification of a stream by humans. The three main forms of stream modification are impoundments, water removal or diversion, and channelization.

Channelization is modification of a stream channel to make it more uniform. It involves a number of different activities: channel straightening, ditching or dredging, snagging, and diking. It is done to decrease the lateral extent of streams and to increase flow (thereby reducing flooding tendency). Channelization is the most pervasive anthropogenic disturbance to streams. Virtually all streams in eastern North America - from small creeks to the largest rivers - have been channelized in some way.

Channelization simplifies the structure of the channel by straightening the mainstem, reducing meandering, and increasing the slope of the stream. This results in higher flow velocities, higher erosional energy, and downcutting of the channel. Downcutting causes the stream to become entrenched (more confined to its mainstem), thus preventing further meandering. The length of the stream is generally reduced. Shorter stream length over the same upstream to downstream elevation difference as before channelization, means an increase in the stream gradient and flow velocity. The shorter length and increased velocity impacts organic-matter processing, nutrient spiraling and other important processes. Short, channelized reaches also offer less habitat for biota than longer, sinuous stream reaches.

The unnatural channel configuration also does not dissipate energy as efficiently. During high-flow conditions, channelized reaches usually experience increased erosion and scouring of the bank. Increased erosion and bank instability often result in increased suspended sediment and bed load carried to downstream areas. Increased sediment transport results in increased sedimentation downstream (aggradation), which disturbs habitat and increases flooding potential. Turbidity is often higher in channelized sections than natural stream reaches, which can have negative impacts on the primary production, water quality, and food supply to stream organisms.

Altered stream velocities affect the sediment particle size distribution along the stream bed. There is often a loss of organic materials, either from their physical removal during channel dredging, or from increased water velocities following channelization. Since the channel configuration is more uniform, lacking pools, riffles, and other diverse morphological characteristics, the substrate also tends to become more uniform. The lack of structures to encourage trapping and deposition of sediment also results in reduced habitat diversity.

Channelization also lowers the water table in the adjacent floodplain and reduces the exchange between hyporheic and surface flow in the stream. As the stream velocity increases and downcutting of the channel occurs, there is a resultant lowering of the mainstem water table. Lowering of the water table causes less water to be stored in the floodplain, which deprives lateral habitats of their source of cool groundwater.  As groundwater drains out of the surrounding land, the soil is compacted, further reducing the capacity of the soil to store water. As the flow of groundwater into the stream is reduced, groundwater (hyporheic) recharge from the stream is also reduced.

The disturbance associated with channelization impacts fish and other stream life by reducing instream and streamside vegetation; disrupting the riffle-pool sequence; reducing overall stream length; increasing gradient and flow velocity; dewatering adjacent lands; and changing the basic physicochemical regime.

A common feature of channelization is snagging - the removal of tree stumps and other large woody debris. Studies indicate that the presence of large woody debris in streams is one of the most important factors regulating stream habitat diversity. Large tree falls obstruct stream flow and allow for the trapping of coarse substrate above the obstruction (e.g., gravels important to fish spawning) and scouring of the streambed downstream of the obstruction, leading to pool development. Woody debris provides cover and shelter for fish, and in some cases fish spawn in the log cavities. Removal of large woody debris during channelization can also reduces invertebrate abundance and diversity.

Riparian vegetation - the source of instream woody debris - is typically removed when streams are channelized. Removal of canopy vegetation reduces shading, resulting in increased stream temperatures. Leaf supply may also be disrupted. The increased light may stimulate greater primary productivity, however. Trees along a stream and their root systems play an important role stabilizing the stream bank. When removed, the banks become more susceptible to erosion. Removal of riparian vegetation also reduces the ability of the floodplain to attenuate flood waters.

The combination of increased flow velocities and bank erosion causes more fine sediment to be carried into the stream than the stream can handle, and instream substrate to become more unstable. Loose and shifting substrate prevents benthic invertebrate communities from becoming established and reduces coarse spawning substrates for fishes. The increased number of sediment particles in transit in the water has an abrasive effect on the gills of fish and macroinvertebrates. Interstitial spaces in the substratum become clogged, removing an important retreat for macroinvertebrates from high current velocities, as well as further reducing stability of the stream bed.
