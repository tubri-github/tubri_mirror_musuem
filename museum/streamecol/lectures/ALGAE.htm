STREAM ALGAE

Algae are a ubiquitous group of organisms. All algae are closely related on a functional level; they are autotrophs, responsible for most of the photosynthesis in streams. However, algae are diverse taxonomically. Algae are classified in three different kingdoms and a number of different divisions (= phyla of animal classification).

All algae have chlorophyll a and lack multicellular gametangia. They have simple organization, existing either as single cells, colonies, or strands of cells.


Taxonomic classification

Algae are classified in as many as 10 different divisions. Classification of algae is based on 1) pigmentation, 2) chemistry and structure of internal storage products, 3) cell wall composition, and 4) flagellation. A classification is presented below for groups of algae found in streams. The groups are listed in order of decreasing importance.

Diatoms are classified in Division Bacillariophyta of Kingdom Protista. They have chlorophylls a and c, with carotenoids that make them appear brown or golden in color. They are probably the most widespread and abundant of all algae in streams. They have silicious cell walls in two halves with a slit in the middle that allows them to be motile.

Green algae are classified in Division Chlorophyta, often in Kingdom Plantae (although most consider them to be protists). They mainly have chlorophylls a and b. The cell wall is made of cellulose and pectin. They are green in color and usually filamentous. Growths of filamentous green algae may be extensive in well-lit streams with course bottoms.

Blue-green algae are classified in Division Cyanophyta of Kingdom Monera (bacteria). They are also called cyanobacteria, and are the only procaryotic algae. They have chlorophyll a and phycobilins and appear blue-green to olive in color. They occur as mats or filaments.

Yellow-green (or golden) algae are classified in Division Chrysophyta of Kingdom Protista. They have chlorophylls a and c, are yellow-green in color, and have cell walls of cellulose and pectin. Only a few genera of yellow-green algae occur in streams and all are filamentous.

Red algae are classified in Division Rhodophyta of Kingdom Plantae. They have chlorophyll a and phycoerythrin. They are olive to maroon in color. Most red algae are marine, but several filamentous groups occur in streams.


Ecological classification

Algae may also be classified into different ecological categories. They are typically attached to something and are most often referred to as a component of periphyton. However, this term technically means "on or around plants" and should be reserved for organisms growing on vascular plants. Included among "periphyton" are various microscopic and short-filament algae, microbes, animal protists and meiofauna. All of these organisms, grow on a variety of substrates (living and nonliving). Thus, it is becoming more common to use different terminology to refer to organisms growing on different types of substrates. Another term for algae and other organisms growing on plants is epiphyton. When attached to animals, algae and other organisms are called epizoon. When attached to rocks, they are called epilithon. When attached to wood, they are sometimes referred to as epidendron. Episammon refers to organisms growing on sand. Epipelon refers to organisms attached to fine sediment.

There is considerable microhabitat specificity. For example, epipelic communities are dominated by motile diatoms that can move over and between fine sediment particles; whereas, epilithon is dominated by non-motile (attached) diatoms and filamentous algae. Physiognomy refers to the physical structure of a community. Algae can occur in motile, nonmotile or prostrate forms. They can be attached by mucilagenous pads or stalks. They can occur as short filaments or long filaments that extend above the surface of the substrate.

 

Ecological role

First and foremost, algae are producers that provide an important, fresh, fixed carbon source to herbivores. Algae is nutritionally superior to detritus (dead organic matter). Algae also produce oxygen for aerobic organisms, and are useful indicator organisms. Benthic algae community composition is reflective of water quality. For example, dominance of algal communities by blue-green algae is generally a sign of excessive nutrient enrichment.

Biomass and pigments

Biomass determination is an important way of assessing a particular taxon's importance in a community or a community's importance to an ecosystem. Measurement of benthic algal biomass is complicated by the fact that benthic algae are a component of a community that includes bacteria, fungi, and protozoans.

Although the most important energy source for streams is allochthonous dead organic matter, autochthonous, autotrophic production can be substantial. Already mentioned that algae are superior to detritus as a food source. Modeling and empirical studies suggest that lotic algal biomass can support 10-20 times more grazer biomass than detritus. So the algal biomass one measures in a stream at any particular time is not a good indication of the herbivore biomass it can support.

Not all algal biomass produced in an area is consumed by herbivores. Laboratory studies suggest that a large amount of the algae grazed by snails is transported downstream. Sloughed algal biomass can be an important detrital source for filtering heterotrophs.

Short-term changes in algal biomass can be used as an index of algal productivity. However, one must be careful to account for grazing, upstream inputs, and downstream losses.

There are three main approaches to determining algal biomass. Ash-free dry mass calculation involves drying a collected sample to constant weight, oxidizing in a muffle furnace, and measuring the loss in weight due to oxidation. This provides a measure of total organic matter in a sample, but it doesn't allow one to distinguish algal biomass from other periphyton, and it doesn't account for the physiological state of the organic matter (dead or living).

Pigment analysis involves extracting pigments from collected samples and measuring each pigment's absorbance of light of different wavelengths. Because different pigments absorb maximally at different wavelengths, pigment analysis provides clues as to the types and concentrations of pigments present. This allows differentiation of algal biomass from other organic biomass.

Biovolume measurement involves measuring the dimensions of algal cells under a microscope and calculating cell volumes for each species. When included with determination of algal community composition, this method provides information on biomass for each algal species. However, this is very labor intensive and it is difficult to distinguish live cells from dead cells under the microscope.
