LEAF AND OTHER CPOM BREAKDOWN

The primary source of energy for streams (especially small streams in forested regions) is leaves and other CPOM from the surrounding riparian vegetation. In temperate regions, most of the leaf fall occurs in autumn when deciduous trees lose all of their leaves. However, a small amount of leaf fall or leaf wash-in occurs throughout the year.

Dead leaves entering streams in fall have low nutritional value (lack most of the nutrients present in green leaves). From the time they enter streams, leaves undergo a predictable cycle of breakdown that allows them to be utilized by animals. In upland streams with course substrates, leaves get caught among rocks and form packs (leaf packs). In lowland streams, leaves form large deposits in pools (leaf beds).

Within 1 or 2 days of entering the stream, the remaining nutrients and secondary compounds are leached out. The material left (cellulose and lignin) is difficult for animals to digest. The leaves are first colonized by bacteria and fungi. These organisms secrete enzymes that begin to digest the leaf (microbial conditioning). Two weeks of microbial conditioning would be sufficient to cause the leaves to fragment. However, shredders colonize before this time and tear the leaves into pieces (ortis) in the process of eating the bacteria and fungi. The ortis then serves as food for collectors.

All leaves do not break down at the same rate. Rates vary among tree species based mainly on physical and chemical properties of leaves (Leaf breakdown, Figure 27.1). These properties may increase conditioning time or the ability of microbes and shredders to colonize leaves. The different breakdown rates help to ensure that leaf matter will be available for a longer portion of the annual cycle than would be the case if all breakdown rates were the same.

Leaf packs in coarse bed streams breakdown complete over the course of a year and are washed away in scouring spring floods.  Leaf beds persist longer.  The leaves may become fragmented, but the ortis can remain in beds for a number of years and be layered over by new leaves.

Branches and trunks of trees (woody debris) take even longer to breakdown.  Wood debris contains more lignin (a natural polymer which gives wood its woody consistency) than leaves, and is more resistant to microbial decomposition. Branches form brush piles along the sides of gradient areas and at bridge supports in streams.  Tree trunks form log and debris dams in small streams and log jams or wicker dams in large rivers.  Breakdown is a function of the degree of submersion of the woody debris: the more water-soaked the wood; the quicker it breaks down.   However, large wicker dams have been know to persist for centuries and if the wood becomes buried in sediment it can mineralize and persist even longer.  Some woods are naturally water-resistant (e.g. cypress and certain conifers).   

Just like on land, woody debris submerged in streams (especially small streams) is colonized by fungi, mosses and even freshwater sponges, which accelerate the breakdown process.
