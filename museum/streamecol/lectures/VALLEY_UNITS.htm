STREAM VALLEY AND CHANNEL UNITS

Last time we considered general physical attributes of catchment basins, concluding with a discussion of the dimensionality of streams. I mentioned that there are physical, chemical and biotic gradients along each dimension, with gradual changes in conditions (continua) along each gradient.

We now take a more detailed look at the physical habitats occurring within a stream system, examining the different types of valley segments, stream reaches and channel geomorphic units typically recognized. As with any classification system, what we are doing in classifying stream habitats this way is collapsing the complexity and variety of nature into a limited number of categories. However, the categories do have some basis in reality because they represent common responses of the landscape to the physical forces of water flowing through a catchment system.

Authors of text make the point that the practice of classifying stream habitats based on hydrological and geomorphic factors is relatively new and still undergoing refinement. They present two classifications schemes: one focused at the level of valley segments and stream reaches; and the other focused at the level of channel geomorphic units.

Valley Segments

A valley - one of the two main subdivisions of watersheds (catchment basins) - is the area where water and sediment collect and within which the stream resides. Three different valley-segment types are recognized based on sediment input and transport mechanisms: colluvial, alluvial and bedrock. A colluvial valley segment is an uncommon valley type in which the valley floor is filled with material delivered by mass wasting of hillslopes (e.g., landslides). Alluvial valley segments are valleys filled by sediment transported by stream flow (the material tends to be more mobile). Bedrock valley segments are dominated by bedrock, with very little sediment.

Valley segments are said to be supply-limited (more or less) depending on the amount of sediment delivered to them, and transport-limited (more or less) depending on the ability of water to move sediment within the valley. Bedrock valley segments have very little sediment in them to begin with, and thus tend to be very supply limited.  Any sediment delivered is quickly swept away (not transport limited). Water flowing through colluvial valleys is generally ineffective at removing accumulated sediment. Thus, colluvial valleys are transport limited.  Alluvial valleys are intermediate between these two extremes.

Channel reaches

Channel reaches are relatively homogeneous sections of stream within valley segments. Colluvial valleys have only colluvial reaches; bedrock valleys have only bedrock reaches. However, six different reach types may be seen within alluvial valleys, varying from more supply-limited to more transport-limited extremes, and the boundaries between different reach types are seldom distinct.

The most supply-limited reach types in alluvial valleys are cascade and step-pool reaches. In cascades, particle sizes are large (ensuring limited substrate mobilization) and hydraulic jumps may be high (waterfalls). In step-pool reaches, steps consisting of accumulations of boulders and/or large woody debris, alternate with pools containing finer substrates.

Plane-bed and Riffle-pool reaches are intermediate in terms of the transport/supply-limited continuum, gradient and bed roughness. Plane-bed reaches are characterized by long straight channels of uniform depth. Substrates tend to be uniform, typically with larger cobbles forming a layer of armoring over finer substrates. Riffle/pool reaches are lower in gradient, more variable in terms of depth and substrate composition, and more sinuous in channel form than plane-bed reaches. Riffle/pool reaches become more uniform in high flows.

Regime and braided reaches tend to have the lowest gradients, the finest substrates and the most sinuous (meandering) channel patterns of all alluvial valley reach types. They are typically seen in high order streams. Sediment transport is continuous, and the banks typically are easily eroded. Channel switching is common.

Channel geomorphic units

Channel geomorphic units - also referred to as channel units or habitats - are the smallest of the three units considered here. They tend to be homogeneous and discretely different in depth, current velocity and substrate composition from adjoining units. They are most readily distinguished at low stream flows.

Hawkins et al. (1993) proposed a three-tier system of channel unit classification. The first level distinguishes fast-water from slow water units. Fast water habitats are then subdivided into those with turbulent and non-turbulent flows. Slow-water units (pools) are subdivided into those formed by scour and those formed by dams.

Five types of turbulent (air-bubble entraining) fast-water habitats are recognized depending on gradient, bed roughness, current velocity, and number of hydraulic steps. The types are falls, cascades, chutes, rapids and riffles. Falls are areas of steep vertical drops; whereas cascades have shorter and more stepped drops. Chutes are narrow channels slotted in bedrock. Rapids have relatively steep gradients, but tend to be more planar than cascades. Finally riffles are the lowest gradient of the fast-water habitats and tend to be confined to plane-bed and lower gradient reaches.

Nonturbulent, fast water units are of two types: sheet channels and runs. Sheet channels are areas where shallow water flows over relatively smooth, flat bedrock. They are seen only in bedrock-dominated reaches. Runs are similar but differ in having sand or gravel substrates.

Among slow-water habitats are a number of types of scour pools (eddy, trench, mid-channel, convergence, lateral, and plunge) and impoundment pools (debris dams, beaver dams, landslide dams, backwaters, and abandoned channels). Eddy pools form behind large objects. Trench pools are slow flowing chutes. Mid-channel pools form in areas of flow constriction due to hardened banks or other obstructions. Convergence pools form at the convergence of two streams of equal size. Lateral scour pools form where the stream encounters a resistant stream bank (e.g., outcrop or rip rap). Plunge pools form at the base of a waterfall or cascade.

Dammed pools form upstream of dams. Debris dam pools are dams formed above log jams or piles of debris that happen to clog the streams. Beaver dam pools form above debris dams deliberately constructed by beaver. Landslide pools form when a hillslope fails and blocks stream flow. Backwaters or side channels are found beside the main stream channel, and represent secondary channels that are connected to the stream only at the downstream end. Abandoned channels are former channels that no longer have any connection to the main channel.
