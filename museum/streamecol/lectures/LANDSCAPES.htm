LANDSCAPES AND CATCHMENT BASINS

Streams are formed as by water falling on the land as precipitation and gradually making its way (ultimately) to the sea. Streams flow because the land has relief in the form of mountains, hills, terraces or (stream-cut) canyons.

Water enters streams in two main forms: as runoff (water running over the surface of the land) and as subsurface or hyporheic flow (groundwater). Whether water runs off the surface or enters through the subsurface depends on the permeability or porosity of the land. If all water enters as runoff streams would dry up shortly after rainfall events. The more water that enters the groundwater, the longer it is retained by a watershed (storage), and the longer it charges the streams from below.

When water flows over rocks and soil on land it picks up material (erosion). The faster water flows, the more and bigger the material it can carry (bedload). As the water slows, the material falls out of suspension (deposition; sedimentation; alluviation).

Even within streams, where water flows the fastest, one typically sees the coarsest material (rocks, boulders); where it flows slowly, one typically sees the finest bed material (sand, silt).

Stream ecologists like to think about streams from the standpoint of all of the land they drain (a drainage basin or catchment; Landscape perspective). Catchment = all of the land area the stream is collected from. Streams are thus said to have characteristics of watershed, drainage or catchment area.

Streams range in size from smallest seep or brook to largest river. Within a catchment basin, streams of different size form a network, with small streams connecting to form larger and larger streams. By one method of stream classification (Strahler method), smallest streams (those that typically originate at the highest points of the catchment) are called 1st order streams. These don't receive other streams and are often intermittent. Where two 1st order streams meet, a 2nd order stream forms. Where two 2nd order streams meet a 3rd order stream forms. A stream increases in order only when it receives a stream of like order; order does not change if streams of lower order are added. 1st to 3rd order streams are typically referred to as headwater streams. Streams are no longer wadable when they exceed 5th order. The lower Mississippi River is a 11th order stream.

Streams have four dimensions: a longitudinal dimension (upstream to downstream continuum), a lateral dimension (traces water from channel to adjacent shore), a veritical dimension (down into the substrata), and a dimension of time (seasonal or longer-term changes in conditions).

Physical and chemical conditions change along each of these dimensions, and biotic communities respond to these changes. Longitudinal dimension is the most obvious because it is easiest to view. Longitudinal zonation is term used to describe the very obvious pattern of biotic change along the length of a stream (distinct headwater, intermediate and large stream communities). River continuum concept (RCC) is a conceptual framework that was proposed in 1980 to describe upstream to downstream changes in the physical, chemical and biological attributes of streams.

The basic premise of the RCC is that as a stream increases incident light, nature of organic, photosynthesis and respiration, temperature variability, turbidity, and structure and function of biotic communities change in predictable fashion.

When we modify streams, we upset the continuum (by impounding streams) creating a serial discontinuity (serial discontinuity concept), in which the normal pattern of upstream to downstream change in physical and biological conditions is reset.
