                Macroinvertebrates Defined

Macroinvertebrate=fauna retained by a 500 micrometer net or sieve

                Fauna visible with the unaided eye!

*However, most stream ecologists use a 125-250 micrometer mesh to collect all life stages of invertebrates

  1. Lotic Invertebrates

In lotic (flowing-water) environments, the macroinvertebrate community can consist of hundreds of species.

Arthropods (insects, mites, scuds, crustaceans)

Mollusks (snails, limpets, mussels, and clams)

Annelids (segmented worms)                                                                           

Nematodes (roundworms)

Platyhelminthes (flatworms)

                Most species are found associated with cover (rocks, cobble, fallen trees, snages, leaves, etc.)

               

                BENTHIC- bottom oriented

                Most are not typically found in the water column (due to high predation)

  1. Phylogeny and Adaptations

From where did the stream macroinvertebrate fauna arise?

Insects are terrestrially/freshwater derived (there is only one marine aquatic insect)

                The theory is that insects evolved in terrestrial habitats and secondarily developed a complex life history involving fresh water. The arthropod ancestor to the insects was most likely a marine species.

Mollusks and Crustaceans are derived from marine ancestors (molluscs/curstaceans dominate marine environments)

Aquatic Insects

Most well studied group of benthic macroinvertebrates

Frequently the most abundant large organisms collected in benthic samples

13 orders in North America, but only for the following five do all members pass through an aquatic stage

Odonata (5500 worldwide, 650 NA) (1/3 are lotic and all are predaceous)

Plecoptera (582 NA) (most abundant in cool mountain streams)

                 (Larval stage unknown for 45% of NA species; only known from adults)

Megaloptera (43 NA) (larvae are predaceous and can get quite large)

                 (most common in oxygen rich habitats)

Ephemeroptera (560 NA) (adults may live from 1-2 hours to 2 weeks, most live 1-2 days)

                (winged adults reproduce in mating swarms; mostly males)

Trichoptera (>1200 NA) (case builders: gravel, sticks, sand grains---even used in classification)

                (2/3 of the described species the larval stage is not known)

*****Coleoptera (beetles-5000 aquatic species; i. e. whirlygig beetles) and Diptera (true flies) are also diverse in aquatic environments, but an aquatic stage is not required for many of the species in these groups.  Same is true for Hemiptera (true buigs), Hymenoptera (bees,ants, wasps), Homoptera (leafhoppers).

Trichopetera Megaloptera, Coleoptera, and Diptera are holometabolous

                (larvae does not resemble adult)

Ephemeropetra and Plecoptera are hemimetabolous

 

Adaptations

Insects have invaded lotic environments numerous times and devised a number of different solutions to the problem of living in aquatic habitats

Aquatic environments contain much less oxygen than terrestrial environments. On land insects use a tracheal respiratory system to obtain oxygen from air.

Aquatic insects have evolved a number of different mechanisms for obtaining oxygen:

1. Some obtain oxygen from atmosphere (i.e. snorkeling)

                Some Dipteran larvae have a respiratory siphon. Often live in oxygen poor

                environments

                Rat-tail maggot.

                Other species can pierce plant matter and obtain air from the plant

               

2. temporary storage of an air bubble (i.e. SCUBA)

                Some carry an air bubble down with them (e.g., hemipterans, gyrinid        beetles)

                               

3. Some use respiratory pigments (similar to vertebrate hemoglobin)

                Chironomidae (bloodworms)

4. Most use gills

                Common in virtually all larval stages of aquatic insects

 

How do they maintain their position in streams?

1. sclerotized structures on the legs and body that act as hydrofoils (press the      organsim to the sub.)

2. streamlining of body (Coleoptara)

3. suckers and modified gills (smooth surfaces)

                (Diptera and Coleoptera---this phenomenon is rare)

4. legs and anal hooks (rough surfaces)

                (Megaloptera, Trichoptera, and Coleoptera)

5. silk for attachment (Tricoptera and Diptera)

 

  1. Life History

Lotic environments vary hydrologically, temporally, thermally, and trophically and invertebrate life histories vary accordingly

Some species that inhabit emphemeral streams have dormant eggs

Closely related species that are trophically similar may temporally separate growth and adult emergence in the same stream reach

Life cycle of aquatic insects can vary from a total life cycle of two weeks to 4-5 years

However most have annual life cycles

Typical life cycle

                Eggs deposited

                Eggs hatch soon after deposition

                Larvae grow slowly and reach maturity about one year later (pass through             many instars or molts)

Univoltine: one life cycle per year

Bivoltine: two life cycle per year

Multivoltine: more than 2 life cycles per year

Semivoltine: require 2 or 3 years to complete life cycle

Life history also varies across geographic ranges

Caddisfly: Univoltine in Coastal regions and semivoltine in moutain habitats

 

  1. Mussels

Most are in family Unionidae.  Almost 72% are endangered, threatened, or of special concern (SC). A number of species are extinct.

WHY? Human modifications and biotic interactions

Glochidia - larval stage of mussel

                 Live as ectoparasites on fish

                 Require a specific fish host

Reproduction

1. Females siphon in sperm to fertilize eggs in mantel chamber, release of hundreds of glochidia into water when approached by unsuspecting fish. Glochidia released either as :

                        cloud or packet of glochida

                        conglutinate (a modification of the mantel that resembles a fish)

                        superconglutinate (packet at end of a gelatinous filament)

  1. Crayfish

They are generalists. They process detritus and other vegetative matter and live as scavengers feeding on carcasses of animals.

                Almost 50% are endangered, threatened, or SC
