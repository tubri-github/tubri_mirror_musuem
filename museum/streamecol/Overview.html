Course Overview

Stream Ecology is a course designed for upper level undergraduates and graduate students. The course applies general ecological concepts to stream environments of the southeastern United States. The lecture portion of the course will cover the main topics in stream ecology arranged in several major themes (e.g., physical processes, material transport and storage, etc). Lectures are based primarily of the required textbook, but readings will also be assigned from the primary literature. During the last two weeks of the course students will give presentations on group projects assigned in the beginning of the semester.

The laboratory portion of the course includes two field trips to streams within 1-2 hour driving distance of New Orleans, and a weekend field trip to upland streams several hours distance from New Orleans (Tennessee). These trips will be completed early in the semester to avoid problems with weather and daylight changes. During the field trips, teams of students will be given specific instructions on kinds of samples or data to collect. The samples and data will be processed during the laboratory period in the second half of the semester. Each team will be responsible for summarizing all of the various kinds of data collected.  Teams of two students each will then be given sets of biotic and abiotic data to summarize, analyze, present in class and submit in report form.

Texts:

Hauer and Lamberti 2006. Methods in Stream Ecology. Second Edition, Academic Press

Allan and Castillo 2007. Stream Ecology: Structure and Function of Running Waters. Second Edition, Springer (optional)

Voshell 2002. A guide to freshwater invertebrates of North America McDonald and Woodward (optional)

Grading: Three exams will be given over the course of the semester: two hourly exams during the lecture period, and a comprehensive final exam during the final exam period. Student teams will be graded on group presentations and on reports based on field trips. The written exams will account for 70% of the final grade, with each of the lecture exams counting for 20% and the final counting for 30%. Team presentations and reports will count for 30% of the final grade (15% each). Students who miss field trips will be given make-up assignments. Final letter grades will be assigned based on the following grading scale:

90-100% = A

80-89% = B

70-79% = C
