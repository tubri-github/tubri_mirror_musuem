History
  • Use of living organisms to assess water quality is a century old approach
    • Used widely in Continental Europe
    • US and UK relied on physical and chemical measures
      • Water pollution is essentially a biological problem
  • IBI first done in Illinois
    • Originally designed to test stream quality in an agricultural water shed
    • Look at both inverts and fishes
    • People that helped to create IBI
      • James R. Karr
      • Charles H. Hocutt
      • Isaac J. Schlosser
      • Philip R. Yant
      • Kurt D. Fausch
4
Theory Behind IBI
  • Can be detailed and done over many years or a quick survey of current conditions
  • Based on the premise that pollution tolerance differs among various organisms
  • By looking at the types of organisms that live in the stream and their relative tolerances, you can determine water quality
  • Get into more detail a little bit later
5
Fishes
  • Usually classified down to species level
    • Darters, sunfishes, and suckers are especially important
  • Look at proportion of feeding groups
    • Piscivores
    • Insectivores
    • Omnivores
  • Look at trophic guilds
    • Top predator vs. primary consumer
  • Look at health of fishes collected
    • Black spot  fecal contamination
    • Ectoparasites
6
Fish Sampling
  • Fish are collected in a given reach of a stream
    • Both ends are blocked
    • Used seining and electrofishing for a depletion collection of fish
  • Fish less than 20mm are usually omitted
  • Site selection should be representative of the stream
    • Length of reach depends on stream size
    • Low flow times are preferred
  • Fish species are identified, counted, and assigned to trophic guilds
  • Estimates are created for expected values in order to rate metrics

Quantifying Fish Data
  • IBI measures twelve metrics in three categories (Karr et al. 1986)
    • 1) Species composition
      • Total number of fish species
      • Number of darter species
        • Sensitive to benthic degradation
      • Number of sunfish species
        • Sensitive to pool degradation
      • Number of sucker species
        • Sensitive to chemical degradation
      • Number of intolerant species
        • 5-10% of species most sensitive to degradation
      • Proportion of tolerant species
9
Quantifying Fish Data Cont.
    • 2) Trophic composition
      • Proportion of omnivores
      • Proportion of total insectivores
      • Proportion of piscivores
    • 3) Fish abundance and composition
      • Number of individuals in the sample
      • Proportion of hybrids
        • Sometimes omitted for difficulty
        • An indicator of poor water quality
      • Proportion with disease, tumors, fin damage and skeletal anomalies
10
Quantifying Fish Data Cont.
  • Metrics may have to be added, deleted or altered to effect different local conditions
    • Measuring large river species
    • Evenness
    • Headwater species
    • Reproductive and Functional Guilds
    • Pioneer Species
  • Then ratings given relative to expected results
    • 1: Deviates strongly
    • 3: Deviates moderately
    • 5: Deviates slightly

Six Categories of Total Metrics
  • 60-58: Excellent integrity
  • 52-48: Good integrity
  • 44-40: Fair integrity
  • 34-28: Poor integrity
  • 22-12: Very poor integrity
  • No fish after repeated sampling
12
Assignment of Metric Values
  • Expectation number assignments are based on stream size and geographic region
    • Stream size is measured by Strahler stream order classification system
    • Stream size effects certain metrics (1-4 suggested by Karr)
  • Number of fish species plotted as a function of stream order
    • Should produce a linear regression
    • Line of maximum species richness created and area below this line is trisected to determine scores of 1,3, & 5
    • Data must be based on individual samples

Using Fishes As Biological Indicators
  • Advantages
    • Present in nearly all streams
    • Represent a variety of trophic levels
    • Populations relatively stable
    • Allow for temporal dimension in quality assessment
    • Relatively easy to identify
  • Disadvantages
    • Can only be used in local watershed area
    • Representative sample must be chosen
    • Sampling favors some fish
    • Fish are mobile organisms

EPT & FBI Index
  • EPT: is an enumeration at a broad taxonomic (order) level
    • Specifically looks at proportion of 3 generally intolerant orders of insects
      • E= Ephemeroptera
      • P= Plecoptera
      • T= Trichoptera
  • FBI: Family Biotic Index
    • More detailed at this taxonomic (family) level
    • Gives biotic score according to family

Inverts
  • Depends on how far you classify the organisms according to taxonomic levels
    • Some species that are tolerant in an order or family that is mostly intolerant
  • Examples of intolerant orders
    • Ephemeroptera (Mayflies)
    • Plecoptera (Stoneflies and most intolerant)
    • Trichoptera (Caddisflies)
  • Examples of tolerant orders
    • Diptera (True Flies)
    • Hirudinea (Leaches)

General Overview of Quantifying Invert Data
  • Metrics of Inverts
    • Taxa richness
      • E.g. How many families collected
    • Enumerations
      • Proportions of selected orders
    • Community diversity indices
      • E.g. Shannons index
    • Community Similarity indices
      • E.g. Pinkham-Pearson Index
    • Functional Feeding Groups
      • E.g. Percentage of the shredder functional group
20
Advantages For Using Inverts
  • Ubiquitous, affected by perturbations in all types of water and habitats
  • Large number of species offer a spectrum of responses to perturbations
  • Sedentary nature of many species allow spatial analysis or disturbance effects
  • Long life cycle allows effects of regular or intermittent perturbations, variable concentrations, etc. To be examined temporarily
  • Qualitative sampling and analysis are well developed, and can be done using simple, inexpensive equipment
21
Advantages Cont.
  • Taxonomy of many groups is well known and identification keys are available
  • Many methods for data analysis have been developed for macro invertebrate communities
  • Response of many common species to different types of pollution have been established
  • Macroinvertebrates are well suited to experimental studies of perturbation
  • Biochemical and physiological measures of the response of individual organisms to perturbations are being developed
22
Disadvantages
  • Quantitative sampling requires large numbers of samples, which can be costly
  • Difficult to identify because of small size and because of certain taxonomic groups not well known
  • Factors other than water quality can affect distribution and abundance of organisms
  • Seasonal variation may complicate interpretations or comparisons
23
Disadvantages Cont.
  • Affected by micro and macro habitat changes
  • Propensity of some macroinvertebrates to drift may off set the advantage gained by the sedentary nature of the species
  • Perhaps too many methods for analysis available
  • Benthic macroinvertebrates are not sensitive to some perturbations, such as human pathogens and trace amounts of pollutants
